Minimum Mandatory Triage Items for Suicide Risk
The construction of a defensible, implementable suicide risk triage protocol in ambulatory psychiatry requires the integration of validated clinical instruments with regulatory mandates. The Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS), endorsed by the American Psychiatric Association (APA), the Department of Veterans Affairs, and the Joint Commission, provides the most widely adopted structured framework for this purpose. 
[1-3] The Brazilian Federal Council of Medicine (CFM), through the Código de Ética Médica and Resolução CFM nº 2.217/2018, imposes additional medico-legal documentation and notification obligations that must be layered onto the clinical assessment. 
[4-5] The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE), as interpreted in recent UK consensus statements, emphasizes therapeutic assessment and clinical judgment over sole reliance on risk prediction scales. 
[6-7]

All four frameworks converge on seven minimum mandatory triage items that must be assessed and documented at every encounter where suicide risk is a concern: (1) passive suicidal ideation, defined as a wish to be dead without thoughts of self-harm; (2) active suicidal ideation, defined as thoughts of killing oneself with or without a specific method; (3) presence of a specific suicide plan; (4) intent to act on the plan; (5) access to lethal means, including firearms, medications, and other methods; (6) history of prior suicide attempts or self-directed violence, with attention to method and lethality; and (7) social support and protective factors, including connectedness, access to mental health care, and family involvement. 
[1-2][6][8]

Structured Assessment Using the C-SSRS Cascade
The brief C-SSRS version implemented in ambulatory and emergency settings consists of six cascading questions administered in sequence. Question 1 assesses wish to be dead (passive ideation), Question 2 assesses suicidal thoughts (active ideation), and Question 6 assesses suicidal behavior. Questions 3 through 5 are asked only if Question 2 is positive, and they assess suicidal thoughts with method but without specific plan or intent (Q3), suicidal intent without specific plan (Q4), and suicidal intent with specific plan (Q5). 
[1] At Vanderbilt University Medical Center, C-SSRS scores are aggregated into four risk groups: green (no ideation or behavior), yellow (wish to be dead, thoughts without method, or behavior more than one year ago), orange (thoughts with method but without plan, or behavior 3–12 months ago), and red (intent with or without plan, or behavior within 3 months). 
[1] A recent systematic review and meta-analysis of 27 independent samples confirmed that suicidal behavior assessed by the C-SSRS predicted future non-fatal suicide attempts with a pooled odds ratio of 3.14 (95% CI 1.86–5.31), suicide attempts with a pooled OR of 2.78 (95% CI 1.82–4.24), and suicidal ideation severity with an OR of 1.46 per point (95% CI 1.28–1.77). 
[9] The APA systematic review further demonstrated that current ideation adds predictive power to a history of previous suicide attempt, with the C-SSRS predicting suicide attempts with an OR of 4.8 (95% CI 2.23–10.32) in adolescents and young adults. 
[2]

The clinical decision support logic for operationalizing C-SSRS screening in electronic health records has been validated in a randomized clinical trial, where a 30-day suicide attempt risk probability threshold of ≥2% was used to trigger clinical decision support alerts. Figure 1 from Walsh et al. published in JAMA Network Open illustrates the complete clinical decision support logic linking risk model predictions to C-SSRS screening and clinical actions in ambulatory settings. 
[10]

Comparative Divergences: CFM vs. International Standards
The primary divergence between the CFM framework and C-SSRS/APA/NICE standards lies in three domains. First, regarding screening tools, the CFM does not mandate a specific validated instrument but requires thorough, individualized clinical assessment with comprehensive documentation, whereas the APA and Joint Commission recommend validated tools such as the C-SSRS, Safe-T Pocket Card, or PHQ-9. 
[3][11] Second, regarding referral thresholds, the CFM imposes a legal mandate for immediate referral when active ideation with plan and access to means is identified, requiring documentation of the clinical rationale, whereas the APA relies on clinical judgment supported by tool-based thresholds. 
[2][11] Third, regarding notification, the CFM requires notification of family members or guardians and, in cases of imminent risk, authorities, even without patient consent under Article 46 of the Código de Ética Médica, whereas the APA and NICE leave notification largely to clinical discretion except in cases of imminent danger. 
[4-6]

Clinical and Demographic Risk Factors by Diagnosis
The systematic assessment of clinical and demographic risk factors at every ambulatory follow-up is essential for patients with major depressive disorder (MDD), bipolar disorder (BD), and schizophrenia. The presence of a psychiatric disorder is the strongest single risk factor for suicide, with depression, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia-spectrum disorders each increasing the odds of completed suicide by more than threefold. 
[12] Fazel and Runeson published a comprehensive summary of risk factors for suicide in the New England Journal of Medicine, stratified by quality of evidence, which provides a foundational reference for clinical practice.

In MDD, risk is highest during severe depressive episodes, especially with comorbid anxiety, agitation, or poor impulse control. 
[12-14] In bipolar disorder, mixed affective states—particularly mixed depressive episodes—are associated with markedly increased risk, especially within the first year of illness; suicidal prevalence is greater in BD versus MDD for ideation (29.2% vs. 17.3%), attempts (18.8% vs. 4.78%), and completed suicide (1.73% vs. 0.48%). 
[15] In schizophrenia, risk is heightened during early illness, with clinical predictors including depressive symptoms, young age, male sex, positive psychotic symptoms, and illness insight. 
[12-13] Across all three diagnoses, comorbid substance use disorders, personality disorders (especially borderline and antisocial types), and impulsivity or agitation further increase risk. 
[12-15] A history of prior suicide attempt remains the single most potent predictor of future attempts, with recurrence most likely within 3–6 months of first presentation. 
[13] Demographic risk factors include male sex (higher completion rates), young age at onset, unmarried status, separation or divorce, fewer children, low socioeconomic status, unemployment, family history of suicide or bipolar disorder, and serious adverse childhood events. 
[12][14-16] Protective factors that should be assessed at every follow-up include marriage, co-occurring anxiety disorder, access to mental health care, sense of connectedness, problem-solving skills, spirituality, and employment. 
[8][14]

For adolescents aged 14–17 years, the American Academy of Pediatrics recommends additional screening for ADHD and Autism Spectrum Disorder comorbidity, bullying, discrimination, and sleep disturbances, as these are associated with increased risk. 
[15][17] Depression is present in up to 79% of youths attempting suicide, though it may not always be recognized. 
[16] The C-SSRS risk assessment component is recommended for adolescents, with particular attention to activating events, treatment history, and protective factors. 
[17]

Algorithmic Decision Tree for Risk Stratification
The operationalization of triage items into a stepwise decision tree follows the model endorsed by the Department of Veterans Affairs in its 2024 guideline and the VA/DoD bipolar disorder algorithm. 
[8][18] The flowchart proceeds through ten sequential steps. Step 1 is universal screening using the C-SSRS or equivalent structured questions for all patients. Step 2 assesses for passive or active suicidal ideation; if absent, the clinician proceeds to routine care and documentation. Step 3, triggered by positive ideation, assesses for a specific suicide plan. Step 4 assesses intent to act. Step 5 assesses access to lethal means. The presence of active ideation with plan, intent, and access to means classifies the patient as high acute risk and triggers immediate emergency referral. 
[8][10][19] Step 6 assesses for prior suicide attempts or self-directed violence, which escalates risk to intermediate or high. Step 7 assesses protective factors, which modulate risk downward when present. Step 8 assigns a risk level (high, intermediate, or low). Step 9 defines the action pathway: high risk triggers immediate referral to emergency services (UPA, SAMU, psychiatric hospitalization), safety planning, means restriction counseling, and notification; intermediate risk triggers enhanced follow-up, safety planning, and consideration of referral; low risk continues routine care with ongoing monitoring. Step 10 mandates comprehensive documentation of all assessment findings, actions taken, and communications. 
[8][10][19]

Stepwise Conduct for High-Risk Patients Without Containment
When an ambulatory patient presents with active suicidal ideation, a specific plan, and access to lethal means, the convergence of APA, CFM, and NICE guidelines mandates a defined sequence of actions adapted for settings without containment capacity. 
[6][8][11-12] The clinician must not leave the patient alone and should ensure continuous observation until transfer occurs. Environmental safety measures include removing or securing any available medications, sharp objects, or other potential means of self-harm within the ambulatory setting. 
[8] Emergency services (SAMU, UPA, or psychiatric emergency) must be activated immediately, with clear communication of the urgency and a structured handoff including documentation of risk factors and actions taken. 
[11][17]

Safety Planning Intervention and Means Restriction
The Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention (SPI), endorsed by the Department of Veterans Affairs and listed on the Suicide Prevention Resource Center/American Foundation for Suicide Prevention Best Practices Registry, is the recommended brief intervention to be initiated even in the brief window before transfer. 
[20-21] The SPI consists of six core steps: (1) identification of personalized warning signs for an impending suicidal crisis; (2) internal coping strategies that distract from suicidal thoughts; (3) social distractions and supportive environments (family, friends, safe places); (4) trusted individuals for crisis support, with names and contact information; (5) professional and emergency resources, including crisis hotlines (CVV 188 in Brazil) and local psychiatric emergency contacts; and (6) lethal means counseling, including explicit discussion and documentation of strategies to restrict access to firearms, medications, and other means. 
[17][20-21] The SPI should be delivered collaboratively in approximately 15–30 minutes, with a written copy provided to the patient and included in referral documentation. 
[17][20][22]

The efficacy of the SPI with structured follow-up telephone contact (SPI+) was demonstrated in a quasi-experimental study by Stanley et al. published in JAMA Psychiatry, where SPI+ was associated with a 45% reduction in suicidal behavior over 6 months compared with usual care (odds ratio 0.56, 95% CI 0.33–0.95), and patients in the SPI+ group were nearly twice as likely to attend at least one outpatient mental health visit. 
[20]

Brazilian Legal Framework for Confidentiality and Involuntary Hospitalization
The Brazilian Código de Ética Médica provides the legal basis for confidentiality breach in cases of imminent suicide risk. Article 46 permits breach of medical confidentiality when there is imminent risk to the patient or others, allowing notification of family members, legal guardians, or authorities to protect life, even without patient consent. 
[4-5] Article 18 prohibits breach of confidentiality except as provided by law or when risk to the patient or others is present, and Article 1 reinforces the physician's duty to protect life and health and to avoid malpractice, imprudence, or negligence. 
[5]

Lei 10.216/2001 governs involuntary psychiatric hospitalization in Brazil. Article 6 defines three types of psychiatric hospitalization—voluntary, involuntary, and compulsory—and permits involuntary hospitalization when the patient does not consent and there is risk to self or others, based on a physician's medical assessment. 
[23-24] Article 9 mandates that involuntary hospitalization must be notified to the Ministério Público (Public Ministry) within 72 hours, including the clinical justification and circumstances of admission. 
[23-24] Article 4 guarantees the fundamental rights of patients, including the right to information, consent, and protection against unnecessary hospitalization, requiring that all possible alternatives be considered before involuntary admission. 
[23-24] These provisions are consistent with international standards and ensure oversight through the Public Ministry and judicial review. 
[25-26]

Documentation Requirements and Follow-Up Coordination
The CFM mandates that all elements of the suicide risk assessment be documented in the medical record, including the nature and intensity of suicidal ideation (passive vs. active), presence or absence of plan and intent, access to lethal means, prior suicide attempts (with method and lethality), protective factors, and the rationale for all clinical decisions. 
[4-5] Documentation must also include the specific actions taken to mitigate risk (safety planning, means restriction counseling, environmental safety measures), all communications with family or support persons (including consent or lack thereof), the service contacted for referral (SAMU, UPA, psychiatric emergency), the time and nature of referral, any barriers encountered, and the patient's response to interventions. 
[6][8][11] The APA and NICE recommend structured documentation using validated tools such as the C-SSRS to facilitate communication and continuity of care, and the Joint Commission encourages integration of these tools into electronic medical records. 
[1][3][10]

For patients at intermediate risk (ideation without plan or means, or prior attempts without current intent), the Department of Veterans Affairs recommends enhanced follow-up with increased visit frequency, ongoing safety planning, and reassessment of the safety plan at each encounter. 
[8] For all risk levels, the safety plan should be updated as clinically indicated, and all updates and patient responses should be documented. 
[8][20] For adolescents, the American Academy of Pediatrics recommends adapting the SPI to be developmentally appropriate, involving caregivers in the planning and review process, and addressing unique risk factors such as bullying, discrimination, and comorbid ADHD or Autism Spectrum Disorder. 
[17][22] For elderly patients, clinicians should document comorbidities and polypharmacy if clinically significant, but the core protocol remains unchanged. 
[8]

In summary, the minimum mandatory triage protocol for ambulatory psychiatry without containment capacity integrates the C-SSRS cascade (seven items: passive ideation, active ideation, plan, intent, access to means, prior attempts, protective factors), a three-tier risk stratification (high, intermediate, low), and a defined action pathway for each risk level. High-risk patients require immediate emergency referral, continuous observation, safety planning using the Stanley-Brown SPI, means restriction counseling, notification of family or authorities per CFM Articles 18 and 46, and comprehensive documentation. Involuntary hospitalization, when necessary, must follow Lei 10.216/2001 Articles 6 and 9, with notification to the Ministério Público within 72 hours. This protocol is defensible, implementable, and aligned with the most current evidence from the APA, CFM, NICE, and the Department of Veterans Affairs.

Lithium: Therapeutic Ranges, Monitoring, and Toxicity
Therapeutic Targets and Monitoring Schedule
Lithium remains the gold standard mood stabilizer for bipolar I disorder, recommended as a first-line treatment for acute mania and maintenance by the Canadian Network for Mood and Anxiety Treatments (CANMAT) and the International Society for Bipolar Disorders (ISBD) 2018 guidelines. 
[1] Its narrow therapeutic index demands rigorous laboratory surveillance. The explicit serum lithium target for acute manic or mixed episodes is 0.8 to 1.2 mEq/L, while the maintenance (prophylactic) target is 0.8 to 1.0 mEq/L, as stated in FDA labeling and concordant with CANMAT 2018 and Kaplan & Sadock's 12th edition. 
[1-4] For elderly patients or those with increased sensitivity, expert consensus supports a lower maintenance target of 0.6 to 0.8 mEq/L. 
[5-6] All blood samples must be drawn as 12-hour post-dose trough levels to ensure accuracy. 
[2][5]

Before initiating lithium, baseline screening must include serum creatinine and estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) and free T4, serum calcium, electrolytes, complete blood count (CBC), and pregnancy testing in women of childbearing potential. An electrocardiogram (ECG) is recommended for patients over 40 years of age or those with cardiovascular risk factors. 
[1-4] During the initiation phase (first 3 to 6 months), serum lithium concentrations should be obtained 5 to 7 days after each dose adjustment, then every 1 to 2 weeks until clinical and laboratory stability is confirmed. Renal function should be reassessed at 1 month and 3 months; thyroid function and serum calcium at 3 months; and electrolytes at 1 to 3 months. 
[1-4][6] During the maintenance phase (beyond 6 months), serum lithium should be checked every 3 months during the first year, then every 6 months thereafter. Renal function (creatinine, eGFR) should be monitored every 6 months, thyroid function every 6 to 12 months, serum calcium and electrolytes annually, and ECG and CBC only as clinically indicated. 
[1][6-7] The typical starting dose for adults is 300 mg three times daily of lithium carbonate, titrated every 3 days based on serum levels, with usual maintenance doses of 600 mg two to three times daily. 
[2-3]

Toxicity Grading and Clinical Alerts
Lithium toxicity is graded as mild (1.5 to 2.0 mEq/L), moderate (2.0 to 2.5 mEq/L), and severe (greater than 2.5 mEq/L). Mild toxicity presents with fine tremor, mild polyuria, transient nausea, lightheadedness, and muscular weakness, and is managed by dose reduction or cessation. Moderate toxicity manifests as drowsiness, hyperreflexia, ataxia, blurred vision, tinnitus, slurred speech, vomiting, diarrhea, and ECG changes, requiring cessation and supportive care. Severe toxicity is characterized by seizures, coma, cardiac arrhythmias, hypotension, renal failure, and risk of irreversible brain damage; hemodialysis is the treatment of choice. 
[2-5] Toxicity may occur even within the therapeutic range in elderly or renally impaired patients, and may be precipitated by dehydration, hyponatremia, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, or diuretics. 
[2-4]

Mandatory clinical alert flags before applying the standard lithium protocol include renal impairment (eGFR 30 to 89 mL/min: start at lower doses, titrate slowly with frequent monitoring; eGFR less than 30 mL/min: lithium is contraindicated), women of childbearing age (pregnancy testing mandatory, increased monitoring during pregnancy due to altered renal clearance), and pre-existing cardiovascular disease (baseline ECG mandatory, repeat if symptoms of conduction abnormality arise). 
[1-4]

Valproate: Monitoring and Teratogenicity Alerts
Valproate (divalproex, valproic acid) is a first-line mood stabilizer for bipolar disorder and is also used in epilepsy. The therapeutic serum concentration target is 50 to 100 µg/mL. 
[8-9] Baseline laboratory tests prior to initiation must include liver function tests (ALT, AST), CBC with platelets, serum ammonia, and coagulation parameters if hepatic dysfunction is suspected. A pregnancy test is mandatory for all women of childbearing potential. 
[1][8-9] During the initiation phase (first 6 months), serum valproate levels should be measured 1 to 2 weeks after starting therapy, then every 1 to 3 months. Liver function tests and CBC with platelets should be repeated at the same intervals, as this period carries the highest risk for idiosyncratic hepatotoxicity and thrombocytopenia. 
[1][8-10] During the maintenance phase (beyond 6 months), liver function, CBC with platelets, and serum valproate levels should be assessed every 6 to 12 months, with annual monitoring sufficient in stable, uncomplicated patients. A systematic review supports the dispensability of annual laboratory follow-up after more than 2 years of uncomplicated valproate use, provided there have been no dose adjustments or co-medication changes. 
[10]

The teratogenicity alert for valproate is critical. The rate of major congenital malformations with valproate exposure is approximately 9%, rising to 25% at doses above 1,450 mg/day, and is approximately four times higher than with other antiseizure monotherapies. 
[8][11-13] The American Academy of Neurology (AAN), American Epilepsy Society (AES), and Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine (SMFM) guidelines mandate avoidance of valproate in women of childbearing potential whenever clinically feasible. 
[14][14-15] If valproate must be used, clinicians must document risk counseling, co-prescribe effective contraception, implement a pregnancy prevention program, and prescribe folic acid supplementation of at least 0.4 mg daily. 
[14][14][16-17] In the Brazilian regulatory context, these requirements are harmonized with international standards through ANVISA, though no specific CFM/ABP resolution numbers for this protocol have been identified in the literature. 
[18-19] Mandatory clinical alert flags include hepatic disease (absolute or relative contraindication depending on severity), women of childbearing age (regulatory restriction, specialist review), and dose adjustments or new comorbidities (revert to initiation-phase monitoring frequency). 
[1][8]

Carbamazepine: Monitoring and Cardiovascular Alerts
Carbamazepine is a first-line anticonvulsant and mood stabilizer with a therapeutic serum concentration range of 4 to 12 µg/mL. 
[21-22] Baseline laboratory tests must include CBC with differential and platelets, liver function tests (AST, ALT, bilirubin), serum sodium, renal function (BUN, creatinine, urinalysis), and carbamazepine serum level. HLA-B1502 genotyping is recommended in patients of Asian descent to assess the risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome. 
[1][21][23-24] Baseline ECG is indicated for patients with pre-existing cardiovascular disease or risk factors for conduction abnormalities, as carbamazepine can cause AV block and arrhythmias. 
[21][23][25]

During the initiation phase, CBC and liver function tests should be repeated every 2 weeks for the first 2 months, then monthly until the end of the initiation phase. Serum sodium and renal function should be checked monthly. Carbamazepine serum levels should be measured after reaching a stable dose (typically within 3 to 7 days of dose adjustment) and periodically thereafter. 
[1][21-24] Adult dosing typically starts at 200 to 400 mg/day, titrated upward to a usual maintenance dose of 800 to 1,200 mg/day, with rare cases requiring up to 1,600 mg/day. 
[22][24] During the maintenance phase, CBC, liver function tests, and serum sodium should be checked every 3 to 6 months; renal function every 6 to 12 months; and carbamazepine serum levels every 6 to 12 months or sooner if there are changes in clinical status, suspected toxicity, or addition of interacting medications. 
[1][21-23] Thyroid function tests may show decreased values with carbamazepine, though routine monitoring is not universally recommended unless clinically indicated. 
[21][24]

Mandatory clinical alert flags include pre-existing AV block, arrhythmias, or heart failure (baseline ECG and specialist review required), new cardiovascular symptoms or ECG changes during treatment (urgent ECG and assessment), and drug-drug interactions, as carbamazepine is a potent hepatic enzyme inducer. 
[1][21][23-25]

Antipsychotics: General and Agent-Specific Protocols
General Atypical Antipsychotic Protocol
All atypical (second-generation) antipsychotics share a class risk for metabolic disturbances, including weight gain, dyslipidemia, and impaired glucose metabolism. The CANMAT 2018 guidelines and consensus statements recommend baseline measurement of weight, body mass index (BMI), waist circumference, blood pressure, fasting glucose or HbA1c, and fasting lipid profile for all patients initiating an atypical antipsychotic. 
[1][26-28] These parameters should be reassessed at 12 weeks (3 months) and then annually during the maintenance phase. 
[1][26-27] Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS), including acute dystonia, parkinsonism, akathisia, and tardive dyskinesia, should be evaluated at every visit during the initiation phase and after dose increases, then every 6 to 12 months during maintenance, with increased frequency (every 3 months) for older patients or those on high-risk agents. 
[26][28-29] Monitoring for neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) is recommended during the early phase of treatment and after dose changes, with immediate assessment if clinical suspicion arises. 
[26][29-30]

Marder and Cannon published a widely referenced monitoring framework in The New England Journal of Medicine that specifies the timing and frequency of side-effect evaluations for antipsychotic medications.

Routine prolactin monitoring is not universally required for all atypical antipsychotics but should be considered if the patient develops symptoms suggestive of hyperprolactinemia (galactorrhea, amenorrhea, sexual dysfunction), or if risperidone or paliperidone is used. 
[29][31-33] Real-world adherence to metabolic monitoring guidelines remains suboptimal, with a meta-analysis by Mitchell et al. finding that baseline glucose was measured in only 44.3% and cholesterol in 41.5% of patients prescribed antipsychotics, underscoring the importance of systematic protocols. 
[34-35]

Agent-Specific and Clozapine Protocols
The two-tier protocol requires agent-specific conditional branches for the highest-risk outliers. Olanzapine and clozapine carry the highest metabolic risk among atypical antipsychotics. For olanzapine, enhanced metabolic monitoring (fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid profile, weight, BMI, waist circumference) is mandatory at baseline, 3 months, and annually. Liver function tests (ALT, AST, GGT) should be monitored at baseline and periodically, as olanzapine is associated with asymptomatic transaminase elevations (ALT greater than 200 IU/L in 2% of patients in premarketing studies). 
[36-37] Patients with a history of low white blood cell count or drug-induced leukopenia/neutropenia require frequent CBC monitoring during the first few months, with discontinuation at the first sign of clinically significant WBC decline. 
[37-38]

Clozapine requires the most intensive monitoring of any antipsychotic due to the risk of severe neutropenia (agranulocytosis). The absolute neutrophil count (ANC) must be obtained at baseline; clozapine initiation is not recommended if baseline ANC is less than 1,500/µL. ANC monitoring is required weekly for the first 6 months, biweekly for the next 6 months, and monthly thereafter. 
[1][27][39-40] The risk of severe neutropenia is greatest during the first 18 weeks of treatment. 
[39] Enhanced metabolic monitoring is also required, with baseline and periodic assessment of weight, BMI, waist circumference, blood pressure, fasting glucose, and lipid profile. Liver function tests and ECG are recommended at baseline and as clinically indicated, with vigilance for myocarditis (especially in the first month) and hepatotoxicity. 
[39-40] Most clozapine adverse effects emerge within 3 months, and almost all appear within 6 months after initiation, with notable exceptions including weight gain, diabetic ketoacidosis, cardiomyopathy, and seizures. 
[40]

Quetiapine carries a risk for QTc prolongation, particularly in patients with pre-existing cardiovascular disease or those on concomitant QT-prolonging medications. Baseline ECG is recommended for patients with risk factors, and repeat ECG should be performed after dose increases or if symptoms suggest arrhythmia. 
[41-44] Standard metabolic monitoring applies as described above. 
[41-42]

Risperidone and paliperidone are notable for sustained prolactin elevation, which may result in galactorrhea, amenorrhea, sexual dysfunction, and rarely prolactinomas. Baseline prolactin measurement is recommended if clinically indicated, and periodic assessment is warranted in symptomatic patients or those on long-term therapy, with gender-specific monitoring as women are more likely to experience prolactin-related adverse effects. 
[31-33][45] Both agents also require standard metabolic monitoring. 
[31][45]

For first-generation (typical) antipsychotics, the monitoring protocol emphasizes EPS assessment at every visit during initiation and after dose increases, tardive dyskinesia screening every 6 to 12 months (every 3 months in older adults), and baseline and periodic metabolic and cardiac monitoring as described for atypical agents. 
[26][28-29][43-44][46-47]

DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria and Bipolar Differentiation
Episode Criteria and Mixed Features Specifier
The American Psychiatric Association defines a major depressive episode (MDE) as the presence of five or more symptoms during the same two-week period, with at least one being depressed mood or anhedonia. The remaining symptoms include significant weight change or appetite disturbance, insomnia or hypersomnia, psychomotor agitation or retardation, fatigue, feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt, diminished concentration, and recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation. The episode must cause clinically significant distress or functional impairment and must not be attributable to substance effects or another medical condition. 
[1-2] For adolescents aged 14–17, the DSM-5-TR permits irritability to substitute for depressed mood, and failure to make expected weight gain satisfies the appetite/weight criterion; however, the fundamental five-symptom threshold remains unchanged. 
[2-3]

A manic episode requires a distinct period of abnormally elevated, expansive, or irritable mood with increased energy lasting at least seven days (or any duration if hospitalization is necessary), accompanied by three or more characteristic symptoms (four if mood is only irritable): grandiosity, decreased need for sleep, pressured speech, flight of ideas, distractibility, increased goal-directed activity, and excessive involvement in risky activities. The episode must cause marked impairment, necessitate hospitalization, or include psychotic features. 
[1][5-6] A hypomanic episode shares the same symptom profile but requires only four consecutive days, must represent an observable change in functioning, and by definition does not cause marked impairment, hospitalization, or psychosis. 
[1][5-6]

The mixed features specifier, introduced in the DSM-5, can be applied to episodes of major depression, mania, or hypomania. For an MDE with mixed features, at least three manic/hypomanic symptoms must be present during the majority of days of the episode, must be observable by others, and must represent a change from usual behavior. 
[1][7] Maintaining the three-symptom threshold is supported by recent evidence demonstrating that lowering it to two symptoms triples prevalence without improving clinical validity or predictive value for bipolar disorder. 
[8] For manic or hypomanic episodes with mixed features, at least three depressive symptoms (dysphoria, diminished interest, psychomotor retardation, fatigue, worthlessness/guilt, or suicidal ideation) must be present. 
[1][7] Hospitalization is indicated when outpatient presentations cross into severe suicidality, psychosis, catatonia, or functional incapacity during any episode type. 
[1][3]

Distinguishing BD-I from BD-II in Outpatient Settings
Bipolar I disorder (BD-I) requires at least one lifetime manic episode; depressive episodes may occur but are not required for diagnosis. Bipolar II disorder (BD-II) requires at least one hypomanic episode and at least one major depressive episode, with no history of mania. 
[1][5-6] BD-II is not a milder illness: patients spend substantially more time in depressive states (depressive-to-hypomanic episode ratio up to 39:1), experience significant functional and cognitive impairment, and carry a suicide risk equivalent to BD-I. 
[9] Psychotic features during mood elevation automatically classify the episode as manic and the diagnosis as BD-I. 
[1][10]

The most common diagnostic pitfall is misdiagnosis of BD-II as major depressive disorder (MDD), with an average diagnostic delay exceeding 10 years. 
[9] Clinicians should systematically screen all patients presenting with depression using validated instruments. The Mood Disorders Questionnaire (MDQ) has a reported sensitivity of 80% and specificity of 70%, while the Hypomania Checklist-32 (HCL-32) has a sensitivity of 82% and specificity of 57%. 
[3][7] The Bipolar Spectrum Diagnostic Scale (BSDS) and Internal State Scale (ISS) are additional copyright-free options. 
[3] Corroborative history from family members is invaluable, particularly for BD-II, where hypomanic episodes may be perceived as normal by the patient. 
[1][9] Features suggesting bipolar disorder in a depressed patient include early onset, hyperphagia, hypersomnia, psychotic features, family history of bipolar disorder, frequent affective episodes, and inadequate response to antidepressants. 
[6-7]

Treatment-Resistant Depression: Criteria and Escalation
The CANMAT guidelines define treatment-resistant depression (TRD) as failure to achieve remission after at least two adequate antidepressant trials, where "adequate" requires therapeutic dosing for a minimum of 6–8 weeks with confirmed adherence. 
[19][28] This definition is consistent with FDA and EMA standards, and at least 30% of persons with depression meet this criterion. 
[28] Before diagnosing TRD, pseudo-resistance must be excluded by verifying dose adequacy, treatment duration, and adherence. 
[19][29]

For staging TRD severity, the Maudsley Staging Model (MSM) is recommended for ambulatory use based on Canadian expert consensus, as it incorporates three dimensions—number of failed treatment trials, episode severity, and episode duration—providing superior predictive utility compared to the simpler Thase-Rush hierarchical model. 
[30-32] The Thase-Rush model classifies resistance across five stages from failure of one adequate trial (Stage I) through failure of ECT (Stage V), but does not account for severity or chronicity. 
[33-34] The MSM generates a continuous score using the Maudsley Treatment Inventory for treatment history, CGI for severity, and a duration rating scale. 
[31]

Implementation: Measurement-Based Care Workflow
The American Psychiatric Association recommends systematic measurement-based care (MBC) as the standard for outpatient psychiatric practice, emphasizing digital administration of validated scales, EMR integration, and clinical decision support. 
[41] The PHQ-9 should be administered at baseline and at every follow-up visit, with results documented as discrete data in the EMR. 
[41-42] For mania monitoring, the YMRS should be administered by clinicians at each visit for patients with bipolar disorder. 
[15] The FIBSER scale for adverse effect monitoring is supported by randomized trial evidence as a practical tool for guiding dose adjustments. 
[43]

EMR systems should be configured to deliver assessments prior to appointments via patient portals or text/email, with automated scoring and clinical decision support alerts triggered at PHQ-9 ≥10 (prompting diagnostic evaluation), ≥20 (prompting treatment intensification), and positive item 9 (prompting suicidality assessment). 
[41-42] REDCap, a free electronic data capture platform, has been successfully implemented for digitized MBC pathways, enabling real-time data transfer into EMRs with a 4-fold increase in physician adoption and 3-fold increase in patient enrollment. 
[44] In resource-constrained Brazilian settings, low-cost, scalable digital tools with collaborative governance are essential for sustainable implementation. 
[45]

The recommended monitoring schedule for antidepressant initiation is: baseline assessment with PHQ-9 and clinical interview; first follow-up at 2 weeks; subsequent visits every 4–6 weeks until remission; monthly visits during dose optimization; and every 1–3 months during maintenance. 
[20][22][46] Once remission is achieved (PHQ-9 <5), continuation therapy at the therapeutic dose should be maintained for at least 4–9 months, with gradual tapering thereafter; patients with recurrent or chronic depression may require maintenance treatment for 2 years or longer. 
[4][20][35] Adherence, suicidality, functional status, and emergence of manic/hypomanic features should be assessed at every visit, with explicit documentation and referral triggers for severe, refractory, or diagnostically complex cases.

Organic Exclusion and Workup Protocols
Physical symptoms commonly associated with anxiety disorders—tachycardia, palpitations, dyspnea, chest pain, dizziness, sweating, and tremor—are not pathognomonic for psychiatric conditions and may reflect underlying organic disease. The DSM-5-TR and the American Academy of Family Physicians mandate exclusion of organic causes before assigning a primary psychiatric diagnosis when physical symptoms are prominent, atypical, or occur in patients with risk factors for medical illness. 
[1-3][9] Key organic etiologies to exclude include hyperthyroidism, hyperparathyroidism, pheochromocytoma, cardiac arrhythmias (atrial fibrillation, supraventricular tachycardia), pulmonary embolism, asthma, COPD, seizure disorders, vestibular dysfunction, hypoglycemia, and substance use or withdrawal. 
[1][3][9] Clinical features increasing suspicion for organic etiology include onset after age 45, atypical symptoms (vertigo, loss of consciousness, slurred speech, amnesia), lack of psychological triggers, and poor response to psychiatric treatment. 
[1][9]

The recommended workup protocol is symptom-driven: for tachycardia, obtain ECG, thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4), blood glucose, and urine drug screen; for dyspnea, pulse oximetry, pulmonary function tests, and chest imaging; for chest pain, ECG, cardiac enzymes, and echocardiogram as indicated; for dizziness, ECG, glucose, and neurological assessment. 
[3][9-12] The American College of Cardiology recommends a 10-minute active stand test for tachycardia evaluation, with defined thresholds for orthostatic hypotension (systolic BP drop ≥20 mmHg or diastolic ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes) and POTS (HR increase >30 bpm in adults or >120 bpm during the test). 
[10-11] Medication and substance review is essential, as bronchodilators, sympathomimetics, steroids, caffeine, and benzodiazepine withdrawal can all precipitate anxiety symptoms. 
[1][3-4] Integration of these exclusion protocols into ambulatory triage workflows should employ structured algorithms embedded in electronic health records, with standardized documentation templates capturing orthostatic vital signs, diagnostic test results, and explicit notation of excluded organic causes before psychiatric attribution. 
[13-15]

Burnout: Diagnostic Status and Screening Instruments
Burnout is classified in ICD-11 as QD85 ("Burn-out"), an occupational phenomenon—not a medical disorder—under "Factors influencing health status or contact with health services". 
[31-34] The World Health Organization defines burnout as a syndrome resulting from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed, with three core dimensions: energy depletion or exhaustion, increased mental distance or cynicism related to one's job, and reduced professional efficacy. 
[34-35] ICD-10 codes burnout as Z73.0 ("Problems related to life-management difficulty"), a non-disease category. 
[31-32] The American Psychiatric Association does not include burnout in the DSM-5-TR. 
[1][36] Burnout is not a clinical diagnosis and should not be managed with antidepressant or anxiolytic pharmacotherapy, although comorbid depression or anxiety may require treatment per established guidelines. 
[32][37-38]

Differentiation from major depressive disorder (MDD) and GAD relies on context, symptomatology, and functional impact. Burnout is context-specific (occupational), characterized by helplessness, anger, emotional lability, and loss of empathy, and is typically perceived as reversible with changes in work environment. 
[39-41] MDD is characterized by pervasive sadness, hopelessness, anhedonia, suicidal ideation, and neurovegetative symptoms across all life domains. 
[1][39-40] GAD involves pervasive, excessive worry and physical symptoms not limited to work. 
[1][42-43] Meta-analyses show moderate correlations between burnout and depression (r ≈ 0.52) and burnout and anxiety (r ≈ 0.46), but latent and network analyses confirm they are dissociable constructs. 
[42-43]

The relationship between burnout dimensions and depression/anxiety symptoms in Brazilian healthcare workers is illustrated in Figure 2 from Fischer et al. published in JAMA Network Open, which demonstrates through exploratory graph analysis that burnout domains (emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, personal accomplishment) cluster distinctly from depression and anxiety, supporting the ICD-11 approach of classifying burnout as a separate entity.

PTSD Treatment and Referral Criteria
Pharmacological and Psychotherapeutic Interventions
The American Psychological Association (APA, 2025), the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE, 2018), and the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs/Department of Defense (VA/DoD, 2023) consistently recommend SSRIs and the SNRI venlafaxine as first-line pharmacological treatments for adult PTSD. 
[52-53][53-55] Sertraline (50–200 mg/day) and paroxetine (20–50/60 mg/day) are FDA-approved for PTSD; fluoxetine (20–60 mg/day) is supported by meta-analytic evidence; venlafaxine (75–225 mg/day) is recommended as a first-line alternative. 
[7][52-53][53-54][56] The Cochrane review (2022) found SSRIs improve PTSD symptoms compared to placebo with a risk ratio of 0.66 (95% CI 0.59–0.74), with treatment withdrawal due to adverse events at an absolute rate of 9%. 
[55-56] Second-line agents include mirtazapine (15–45 mg/day) and amitriptyline (75–150 mg/day), based on low-certainty evidence. 
[54-56] Benzodiazepines are contraindicated for PTSD due to lack of efficacy and risk of dependence, cognitive impairment, and worsening of PTSD symptoms. 
[53][53] Cannabis and cannabis-derived products are also recommended against. 
[53][53]

The pragmatic comparative effectiveness trial by Fortney et al. published in JAMA Psychiatry demonstrated that both SSRIs and brief trauma-focused psychotherapy (written exposure therapy) produced clinically meaningful improvement in primary care PTSD patients, with a mean PCL-5 decrease of 14.0 points for SSRIs and 12.1 points for psychotherapy at 4 months (adjusted mean difference 1.79; 95% CI −0.76 to 4.34; P = 0.17).

Referral Criteria for Specialized Psychotherapy
The APA 2025 and NICE 2018 guidelines operationalize mandatory referral to specialized trauma-focused psychotherapy based on the following criteria: moderate to severe PTSD symptomatology meeting DSM-5-TR criteria; availability of clinicians trained in PE, CPT, CT, or EMDR; failure of ambulatory management or non-trauma-focused interventions to achieve clinically meaningful improvement; presence of complex PTSD, severe dissociation, or significant comorbid psychiatric conditions; patient preference for trauma-focused therapy; and safety concerns including suicidality. 
[52-53][53][62] Ambulatory management is appropriate for mild PTSD symptoms with good social support, when the patient declines trauma-focused psychotherapy after informed discussion, or when specialized care is not immediately accessible—in which case evidence-based pharmacotherapy (SSRIs or venlafaxine) should be initiated with ongoing efforts to facilitate referral. 
[52][57] Telehealth delivery of trauma-focused psychotherapy is recommended when validated for the specific protocol and when in-person access is limited. 
[53][53]

Protocol Integration and Recent Updates
For ambulatory triage workflow integration, physical symptom exclusion protocols should employ structured algorithms with clinical decision support systems (CDSS) prompting clinicians to document orthostatic vital signs, symptom correlation, and exclusion of reversible causes before psychiatric attribution. 
[10][12-15] Standardized EHR templates should capture all steps in the exclusion process, including history, physical examination findings, diagnostic test results, and rationale for diagnosis. 
[13-14] Quality assurance mechanisms, including periodic protocol review and clinician training, are essential for maintaining diagnostic accuracy. 
[14]

For brief screening in the Brazilian ambulatory context, the short-version Posttraumatic Diagnostic Scale (PDS, 3 items) is validated in Brazilian Portuguese with excellent diagnostic accuracy (ROC = 0.97, sensitivity 86.4%, specificity 93.5%, Cronbach's alpha = 0.78), offering a highly efficient PTSD screening tool for initial triage. 
[63] The BAT-12 remains the best locally validated brief burnout screening tool, with the BAT4 (4 items) pending formal Brazilian validation but demonstrating cross-national measurement invariance. 
[49-50]

Since 2024, the American Psychiatric Association's DSM-5-TR updates (2024, 2025) have not introduced substantive changes to the core diagnostic criteria for anxiety disorders, OCD, or PTSD, but have clarified the distinction between adjustment disorder and anxiety disorders, reinforcing the need to differentiate transient stress reactions from persistent disorders in ambulatory triage. 
[64-66] ICD-11 continues to define burnout as an occupational phenomenon (QD85), with no changes to its nosological status. 
[67] The ICD-11 introduction of complex PTSD (CPTSD), with additional disturbances in self-organization (affect dysregulation, negative self-concept, interpersonal difficulties), may affect triage decisions, particularly in pediatric and adolescent populations where ICD-11's narrower PTSD criteria result in lower frequency rates compared to DSM-5. 
[68] Clinicians should be aware that some cases may meet DSM-5-TR criteria for PTSD but not ICD-11 criteria, necessitating careful assessment and documentation. 
[68]

DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria and Schneider's First-Rank Symptoms
Diagnostic Criteria for Psychotic Disorders
The American Psychiatric Association (APA) DSM-5-TR defines schizophrenia by the presence of at least two of five core symptom domains—delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior, and negative symptoms—for a significant portion of time during a one-month period, with at least one symptom being delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech. 
[1-3] Continuous signs must persist for at least six months, including at least one month of active-phase symptoms. Functional impairment in work, interpersonal relations, or self-care must be markedly below premorbid levels. Schizoaffective disorder and mood disorders with psychotic features must be excluded, and the disturbance cannot be attributable to substance use or another medical condition. 
[1-2]

Schizoaffective disorder requires an uninterrupted period of illness during which Criterion A for schizophrenia is met concurrently with a major mood episode (depressive or manic). Critically, delusions or hallucinations must be present for at least two weeks in the absence of a major mood episode, and mood symptoms must be present for the majority of the total illness duration. 
[1] Brief psychotic disorder is defined by at least one positive psychotic symptom (delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, or grossly disorganized/catatonic behavior) lasting at least one day but less than one month, with eventual full return to premorbid functioning. 
[1][4] The assessment of cognition, depression, and mania symptom domains is vital for distinguishing among these disorders. 
[1-2]

Clinical Relevance of Schneider's First-Rank Symptoms
Schneider's first-rank symptoms (FRS)—including thought insertion, thought withdrawal, thought broadcasting, auditory hallucinations commenting in the third person, and passivity experiences—are no longer included as core diagnostic criteria in the DSM-5-TR or ICD-11. 
[5-7] The 2015 Cochrane review of 21 studies (6,253 participants) found FRS sensitivity of approximately 57–61.8% and specificity of 74.7–94.1% for schizophrenia, indicating that reliance on FRS would miss approximately 40% of cases and incorrectly diagnose 5–19 per 100 individuals. 
[8] Peralta and Cuesta (2023) confirmed that FRS do not outperform other reality-distortion symptoms in predictive validity against 16 external validators. 
[9] Despite this, phenomenological research demonstrates that FRS are closely associated with self-disorders, which may be more specific to schizophrenia when rigorously assessed. 
[7][10-11] A 2024 expert survey found nearly half of international experts support reintroducing FRS as core criteria, though no clear majority exists. 
[12-13] In clinical practice, FRS remain a useful supplementary indicator—particularly in resource-limited settings—but should not serve as standalone diagnostic markers. 
[8][14]

Extrapyramidal Side Effects and Monitoring Scales
Comparative EPS Risk by Agent and Subtype
Extrapyramidal side effects arise from dopamine D2 receptor antagonism in the nigrostriatal pathway, with risk determined by D2 occupancy, binding kinetics, and serotonergic modulation. 
[15-17] The risk increases substantially when D2 receptor occupancy exceeds 75–85%, with maximum odds ratios ranging from 1.57 for aripiprazole to 7.56 for haloperidol at 30 mg/day. 
[18] Marder and Cannon (2019) published a comprehensive overview of commonly prescribed antipsychotic medications and their side-effect profiles in The New England Journal of Medicine, which provides a practical reference for clinicians selecting agents based on EPS, metabolic, and sedation risk.

The landmark network meta-analysis by Leucht et al. (2013) of 212 randomized controlled trials demonstrated that clozapine produced fewer EPS than all other drugs and placebo (mean ORs 0.06–0.40), followed by sertindole, olanzapine, and quetiapine. 
[19] Haloperidol caused significantly more EPS than all other drugs except zotepine and chlorpromazine. 
[19] Among second-generation antipsychotics, risperidone and paliperidone carry intermediate EPS risk, particularly at higher doses, while aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, and cariprazine have low overall EPS risk but may cause prominent akathisia. 
[19-21] The European Schizophrenia Outpatient Health Outcomes Study found cumulative 36-month EPS incidence of 32.8% for depot typical antipsychotics versus 7.7% for olanzapine, and tardive dyskinesia incidence of 11.1% versus 2.8%, respectively. 
[22] The pooled prevalence of antipsychotic-induced parkinsonism is approximately 20%, akathisia approximately 11%, and tardive dyskinesia approximately 7% across observational studies. 
[23]

McCutcheon et al. (2020) illustrated the relationship between D2-receptor occupancy and clinical outcomes in JAMA Psychiatry, demonstrating the therapeutic window of 60–80% D2 occupancy that balances efficacy with low motor adverse-effect risk.

Monitoring Protocols and Hospitalization Thresholds
The Department of Veterans Affairs guideline recommends monitoring movement status at every follow-up visit during the acute episode and stabilization period. 
[24] The American Academy of Family Physicians recommends AIMS testing at least twice per year for all patients on antipsychotics. 
[25] Marder and Cannon specify that acute EPS should be evaluated at every visit until dose stabilization and after every dose increase, while tardive dyskinesia should be evaluated every 6–12 months (every 3–6 months for high-risk agents or older patients). 
[2] The recommended monitoring protocol uses three validated scales: the Simpson-Angus Scale (SAS) for parkinsonism, the Barnes Akathisia Rating Scale (BARS) for akathisia, and the Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale (AIMS) for tardive dyskinesia. 
[25-28] The American Academy of Family Physicians published a comprehensive dosing and monitoring table in American Family Physician that details monitoring recommendations for antipsychotics, including AIMS testing frequency and laboratory surveillance. 
[25]

During the initiation phase (first 8–12 weeks), AIMS, SAS, and BARS should be administered at baseline and at every outpatient visit. After dose stabilization, assessments should occur at least every 6 months, with every-3-month monitoring for high-risk agents or older adults. 
[2][24-25] Young adults (18–25) are at increased risk for acute dystonia and akathisia, particularly with high-potency agents and rapid dose escalation; lower initial doses and gradual titration are recommended. 
[24][29-31]

Hospitalization is indicated for neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) when sustained hyperthermia (>38.0°C on at least two occasions), severe "lead pipe" rigidity, altered mental status, autonomic instability (tachycardia ≥25% above baseline, blood pressure fluctuation ≥20 mmHg diastolic), and creatine kinase elevation ≥4× upper limit of normal are present. 
[32-35] For severe dystonia, laryngeal involvement with airway compromise, impaired swallowing, or refractory symptoms mandate immediate inpatient transfer. 
[16][35-36]

Clozapine Monitoring Protocol
Hematological and Cardiac Monitoring
Clozapine monitoring follows a structured schedule mandated by the APA, FDA, NICE, BAP, and ANVISA. Prior to initiation, a baseline absolute neutrophil count (ANC) must be obtained; clozapine should not be started if ANC is less than 1,500/μL in the general population or less than 1,000/μL in patients with Benign Ethnic Neutropenia (BEN). 
[24][37-38] The FDA-approved monitoring schedule requires weekly ANC for the first 6 months, every 2 weeks from months 7–12, and monthly thereafter. 
[37-38] The specific dosage modifications and ANC testing frequencies are detailed in the FDA prescribing information: mild neutropenia (ANC 1,000–1,499/μL) requires three-times-weekly monitoring without dose modification; moderate neutropenia (ANC 500–999/μL) requires treatment interruption, daily monitoring, and hematology consultation; severe neutropenia (ANC <500/μL) mandates immediate discontinuation. 
[37-38] All initial reports of ANC <1,500/μL should be confirmed with a repeat measurement within 24 hours. 
[37-38]

The Department of Veterans Affairs guideline recommends obtaining troponin and C-reactive protein (CRP) at baseline and monitoring weekly for at least the first month to detect myocarditis. 
[24] Clozapine dosing begins at 12.5 mg once or twice daily, with gradual titration to 150–225 mg twice daily by two weeks, and a maximum of 450 mg twice daily. 
[37] A recent global Delphi consensus panel supports lowering the ANC cessation threshold to 1.0 × 10⁹/L for the general population and 0.5 × 10⁹/L for Duffy-null individuals, with discontinuation of routine ANC monitoring after 2 years, though this is not yet universally adopted. 
[39] Long-term Finnish data confirm that the risk of agranulocytosis is concentrated in the first 18 weeks but persists at low levels, with an incidence of 2.8 per 10,000 initiators. 
[40-41]

GI Hypomotility and ANVISA Requirements
Clozapine-induced gastrointestinal hypomotility (CIGH) affects 32–75% of patients, with mortality rates for ileus (25–43.7%) far exceeding those for agranulocytosis (2.2–4.2%). 
[42-45] Subjective constipation reporting has very poor sensitivity (18%, 95% CI: 5–40%) for detecting objective hypomotility. 
[46] Bowel function must be assessed at every visit during initiation (weekly) and at least monthly during maintenance. 
[42][47] Universal prophylactic laxative therapy is recommended: docusate 100 mg twice daily plus senna 8.6 mg twice daily, augmented by macrogol 3350 (polyethylene glycol) in refractory cases (the Porirua Protocol). 
[48] Anticholinergic co-prescription must be avoided, as it increases ileus incidence and fatality. 
[42][45][49] Hospitalization is indicated for absence of bowel movements >72 hours with abdominal distension, severe pain, vomiting, or signs of peritonitis. 
[42-43][45][49]

In Brazil, ANVISA mandates registration in the national clozapine monitoring program (Programa Nacional de Monitoramento de Clozapina) before initiation, with prescriber, pharmacy, and patient forms required. 
[50-51] The monitoring schedule aligns with APA/FDA protocols: weekly ANC for 6 months, biweekly for 6 months, then monthly. 
[50-51] Dispensing is contingent on real-time registry compliance—pharmacies cannot release clozapine without confirmation of compliant laboratory reporting from the ANVISA system. 
[50-51] This government-run centralized system differs from the manufacturer-driven Clozapine REMS program in the United States. 
[52] Adverse drug reaction reporting to ANVISA pharmacovigilance and VigiBase is mandatory, though underreporting remains a challenge. 
[50-51] Serum clozapine level measurement is not available in Brazilian public institutions, unlike Chile. 
[51] BEN thresholds are recognized in ANVISA protocols but are not described in Brazilian package inserts. 
[50]

Atypical Antipsychotic Metabolic Risk and Monitoring
Clozapine and olanzapine consistently demonstrate the highest metabolic risk across all parameters. The network meta-analysis by Pillinger et al. (2020) of 100 RCTs (25,952 patients) found mean weight gain differences versus placebo ranging from −0.23 kg for haloperidol to 3.01 kg for clozapine, with total cholesterol increases of 0.56 mmol/L for clozapine and 0.20 mmol/L LDL increase for olanzapine. 
[53] Glucose increases ranged from −0.29 mmol/L for lurasidone to 1.05 mmol/L for clozapine. 
[53] Greater glucose increases were predicted by higher baseline weight (p=0.0015) and male sex (p=0.0082). 
[53] Quetiapine and risperidone carry intermediate metabolic risk, while aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, cariprazine, lurasidone, and ziprasidone demonstrate the most benign profiles. 
[53-55] Smith et al. (2025) confirmed in JAMA Psychiatry that antipsychotic-induced glucose dysregulation does not necessarily track directly with weight gain risk, as lurasidone and quetiapine were associated with significant HbA1c increases despite lower weight gain liability. 
[56]

Monitoring frequency during the initiation phase (first 8–12 weeks) requires weight/BMI/waist circumference at baseline and monthly, fasting glucose/HbA1c at baseline and 12 weeks, and fasting lipid profile at baseline and 12 weeks. 
[2][54-55][57] During stable maintenance (after 12 weeks), weight should be assessed every 3 months for the first year then annually, and glucose/lipids every 6 months for the first year then annually. 
[2][54-55] The olanzapine FDA label specifically warns about hyperglycemia and recommends fasting blood glucose testing at treatment initiation and periodically during treatment. 
[58] Real-world longitudinal data from Feng et al. (2025) demonstrate that metabolic side effects peak within the first year and stabilize thereafter, with clozapine and olanzapine conferring persistently higher cardiometabolic risks. 
[59]

Comparative effectiveness data from Finland, published in JAMA Network Open by Hamina et al. (2024), demonstrate that paliperidone 3-month LAI, aripiprazole LAI, olanzapine LAI, and clozapine are among the best-performing antipsychotics for relapse prevention.

Integrated Protocol Recommendations for Brazilian Practice
For Brazilian outpatient practice, the core oral atypical antipsychotics available through the SUS (Sistema Único de Saúde) are clozapine, olanzapine, risperidone, quetiapine, and ziprasidone. 
[61-62] LAI formulations available include paliperidone palmitate (1-month and 3-month), risperidone LAI, aripiprazole LAI, and olanzapine LAI, though access may be limited by cost and logistics. 
[60][63-64] Low-risk atypicals such as lurasidone, cariprazine, and brexpiprazole have limited SUS availability and are primarily accessible through supplementary health systems. 
[65-66] Risperidone is associated with the highest switching rate in Brazilian cohorts (58.2/100 person-years), while clozapine has the lowest (37.6/100 person-years), reflecting its superior effectiveness in treatment-resistant cases. 
[62] Clozapine confers a survival advantage over non-clozapine atypicals (HR for death 1.21 for non-clozapine users) in Brazilian real-world data. 
[61]

The integrated outpatient monitoring protocol should incorporate the following schedule: during initiation (weeks 0–12), every visit should include EPS assessment (AIMS, SAS, BARS), bowel function inquiry, vital signs, and mental status examination. For clozapine specifically, weekly ANC, weekly troponin/CRP (first month), and weekly bowel function assessment are required. Metabolic parameters (weight/BMI/waist, fasting glucose, lipid profile) should be obtained at baseline and at 12 weeks for all antipsychotics. During maintenance (after 12 weeks), EPS scales should be administered every 6 months (every 3 months for high-risk agents), metabolic parameters every 3–6 months for the first year then annually, and clozapine ANC monthly with continued bowel function assessment at every visit. 
[2][24-25][37][42][53] Young adults (18–25) require lower initial antipsychotic doses, more frequent EPS monitoring during initiation, and preferential use of SGAs with lower EPS risk. 
[24][29-31] All clozapine prescriptions in Brazil must comply with ANVISA registry requirements, with dispensing contingent on real-time laboratory reporting confirmation. 
[50-52]

DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria and ASRS Screening
DSM-5-TR Criteria and Adult Symptom Threshold
The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, Text Revision (DSM-5-TR), published by the American Psychiatric Association, defines adult ADHD as a persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development. 
[1-2] For adults aged 17 years and older, the diagnostic threshold is reduced to at least five of nine symptoms in either the inattention domain (Criterion A1) or the hyperactivity-impulsivity domain (Criterion A2), persisting for at least six months, to a degree inconsistent with developmental level and negatively impacting social, academic, or occupational activities. 
[1][3-4] This adult-specific threshold reflects the recognition that symptom expression attenuates with age while remaining clinically significant. 
[2-3]

Volkow and Swanson, writing in The New England Journal of Medicine, provide a comprehensive overview of the DSM-5 criteria as applied to adults, including specific examples of how ADHD manifests across the lifespan. 
[2] The inattention domain encompasses symptoms such as frequent failure to attend to details, difficulty sustaining attention, seeming not to listen, failure to follow through on tasks, disorganization, avoidance of sustained mental effort, losing necessary items, distractibility, and forgetfulness in daily activities. 
[1][4] The hyperactivity-impulsivity domain includes fidgeting, leaving one's seat, restlessness, difficulty engaging quietly in leisure activities, being "on the go," excessive talking, blurting out answers, difficulty waiting one's turn, and interrupting others. 
[1][4]

The DSM-5-TR specifies three presentations: combined (both A1 and A2 met), predominantly inattentive (only A1 met), and predominantly hyperactive-impulsive (only A2 met), with the term "presentation" replacing "subtype" to reflect developmental instability of symptom clusters. 
[2-3] Additional criteria require that several symptoms were present before age 12 (Criterion B), symptoms manifest in two or more settings (Criterion C), clear functional impairment exists (Criterion D), and symptoms are not better explained by another mental disorder or psychotic condition (Criterion E). 
[1-2][5] Severity specifiers (mild, moderate, severe) and a partial remission specifier are also available. 
[1] Collateral information from informants who have observed the patient across settings is strongly recommended, as self-report alone is prone to recall bias and limited insight. 
[2][5-6]

ASRS v1.1 Screening in Brazil
The Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale v1.1 (ASRS v1.1), developed by the World Health Organization, is the most widely used screening instrument for adult ADHD globally and has been translated and culturally adapted for Brazilian Portuguese. 
[7-8] The American Academy of Family Physicians lists the ASRS v1.1 as the official WHO screening tool for adult ADHD. 
[5] In the original validation by Kessler et al. (2005), the 6-item screener demonstrated sensitivity of 68.7% and specificity of 99.5% in a US community sample. 
[8] In primary care settings, sensitivity approaches 1.0 with specificity of 0.71, a positive predictive value (PPV) of 0.52, and a negative predictive value (NPV) of 1.0. 
[9] The updated DSM-5 version described by Ustun et al. in JAMA Psychiatry achieved sensitivity above 90% using machine learning-optimized item selection. 
[6]

The cross-cultural psychometric analysis by Lewczuk et al. (2024), which included Brazil among 42 countries, demonstrated good reliability and validity with partial measurement invariance, confirming the instrument's applicability in the Brazilian context. 
[7] However, the ASRS v1.1 has important limitations: PPV can be as low as 11.5% in general population samples, and in psychiatric outpatient populations, 54.8% screened positive while only 11.9% had a formal diagnosis. 
[10-11] The optimization of the DSM-5 ASRS screener is illustrated by the area-under-the-curve analysis from Ustun et al. published in JAMA Psychiatry, which demonstrates how the number of screening questions relates to classification accuracy. 
[6]

Pre-Treatment ECG Indications for Stimulant Therapy
The cardiovascular safety of stimulant medications, including methylphenidate (metilfenidato) and lisdexamfetamine (lisdexanfetamina), is a central concern in protocol design. These agents produce mean increases in blood pressure of approximately 2–4 mm Hg and heart rate of 3–6 bpm. 
[13-14] The FDA labeling for lisdexamfetamine specifically cautions against use in patients with known structural cardiac abnormalities, cardiomyopathy, serious cardiac arrhythmia, or coronary artery disease. 
[14]

The guideline consensus, as articulated by the American Medical Society for Sports Medicine, the American Academy of Pediatrics, and NICE, supports a risk-based rather than universal ECG approach. 
[15-16] The American Academy of Pediatrics issued a policy statement that does not support routine ECG before stimulant initiation, contending there is no proof of increased sudden cardiac death rates in patients taking ADHD medications. 
[15] Pre-treatment ECG is indicated when any of the following cardiovascular risk factors are present: personal history of structural heart disease, cardiomyopathy, or serious arrhythmia; family history of sudden cardiac death, inherited arrhythmia syndromes, or cardiomyopathy; unexplained syncope or palpitations at rest; or abnormal findings on cardiovascular physical examination. 
[14-16] For adults without these risk factors, baseline blood pressure and heart rate measurement is sufficient. 
[15][17]

This risk-based logic applies to any stimulant molecule, not solely to metilfenidato or lisdexanfetamina, ensuring protocol durability if additional agents become available. The network meta-analysis by Farhat et al. (2025) in The Lancet Psychiatry confirmed that stimulants were not associated with larger hemodynamic increments compared to non-stimulants such as atomoxetine, reinforcing that cardiovascular monitoring principles are consistent across ADHD medication classes. 
[16] During ongoing therapy, Cortese, writing in The New England Journal of Medicine, recommends that if sustained resting tachycardia exceeding 120 bpm, arrhythmia, or systolic blood pressure above the 95th percentile occurs on two occasions, dose reduction and specialist referral are indicated. 
[17]

The long-term cardiovascular risk associated with cumulative ADHD medication use was examined by Zhang et al. in JAMA Psychiatry (2024), who found associations between increasing cumulative duration of methylphenidate and lisdexamfetamine use and incident cardiovascular disease, though the absolute risk remained low.

Neuropsychological Evaluation Indications
Formal neuropsychological evaluation is not universally required for adult ADHD diagnosis according to major guidelines. The American Academy of Family Physicians emphasizes that a combination of clinical evaluation, screening, diagnostic testing, and collateral information is often sufficient, particularly when there is a personal or family history of ADHD or a childhood diagnosis. 
[5] The European Psychiatric Association's Updated European Consensus Statement similarly supports clinical assessment as the primary diagnostic method, and the UK Adult ADHD Network (UKAAN) does not mandate neuropsychological testing as part of routine diagnosis. 
[19-20]

Neuropsychological evaluation is recommended in the following specific clinical scenarios: when corroborating collateral information is unavailable (no first-degree relative with ADHD, no childhood records, no observer reports); when confounding comorbid mental illness is suspected that may mimic or obscure ADHD symptoms; when malingering or invalid symptom presentation is suspected, particularly in medicolegal or disability assessment contexts; when the cognitive profile is complicated by other neuropsychiatric or medical conditions; and when objective documentation of cognitive impairment is required for disability determination or legal proceedings. 
[5][21-23] Nikolas et al. found that a combined approach using self and informant symptom ratings, positive family history, and a reaction time variability measure correctly classified 87% of cases, supporting the adjunctive value of neuropsychological testing in complex cases. 
[22]

When informant reports are unavailable, clinicians should employ alternative strategies including retrospective review of school, medical, and work records; structured clinical interviews reconstructing the symptom timeline; family history assessment; functional impairment documentation across settings; and validated self-report tools interpreted in clinical context. 
[2][5][24-25] The "or rule" recommended by Sibley (2021) integrates informant reports by considering symptoms endorsed by either the patient or an informant as present, provided they meet the impairment criterion. 
[23] The financial burden and limited accessibility of neuropsychological testing in Brazilian ambulatory settings should be considered, and its use should be reserved for cases where it is likely to impact clinical decision-making. 
[5]

ADHD with Comorbid Bipolar Disorder
In adults with ADHD and comorbid bipolar disorder (TAB), the central concern is mood destabilization. The expert consensus, articulated by Perugi and Vannucchi (2015) and Mucci et al. (2019), mandates that mood stabilization with appropriate mood stabilizers must precede the initiation of any ADHD pharmacotherapy. 
[29-30] Atomoxetine may be effective for ADHD symptoms in bipolar patients but carries a modestly increased risk of (hypo)manic switches when used without mood stabilizers. 
[29-30] When co-administered with mood stabilizers, this risk is reduced, and atomoxetine can be considered safe and effective. 
[29] Bupropion is generally considered to have a lower risk of inducing mania compared to other antidepressants, but evidence in the ADHD-bipolar population is limited. 
[28][31] The hierarchical approach is essential: stabilize mood first with lithium, valproate, or other mood stabilizers; then cautiously introduce atomoxetine (starting at 40 mg/day, titrating to 80–100 mg/day over 2–4 weeks) or bupropion (150–450 mg/day extended-release) with careful monitoring for manic or hypomanic symptoms. 
[29-31] Stimulants may be cautiously reintroduced only after sustained mood stabilization, with close monitoring. 
[29]

ADHD with Comorbid Substance Use Disorder
In adults with ADHD and comorbid substance use disorder (TUS), the primary concern is abuse potential of controlled stimulants. Risk stratification requires distinguishing active substance use (ongoing or within the past 3–6 months, confirmed by self-report, collateral information, or positive toxicology) from sustained remission (abstinence of at least 3–6 months with stable psychosocial functioning). 
[32-33] The American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry recommends a complete psychiatric evaluation including detailed drug and alcohol history, urine drug screening, and collateral information from a spouse, parent, or friend. 
[34]

For patients with active SUD, stimulant therapy is generally contraindicated, and atomoxetine is preferred due to its lack of abuse potential. 
[5][32][35] Atomoxetine reliably improves ADHD symptoms but does not consistently reduce substance craving or use. 
[35] For patients in sustained remission, stimulant therapy may be considered with robust risk mitigation: extended-release or prodrug formulations (lisdexamfetamine, controlled-release methylphenidate) are preferred for their lower abuse potential. 
[5][36-37] The American Academy of Addiction Psychiatry emphasizes risk mitigation measures including controlled substance agreements, prescription drug monitoring program (PDMP) review, random urine drug screening, pill counts, and increased visit frequency. 
[37] The overall cardiovascular risk associated with ADHD medications across age groups, including adults, remains low as demonstrated by the meta-analysis by Zhang et al. published in JAMA Network Open.

DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria and ASRS Screening
DSM-5-TR Criteria and Adult Symptom Threshold
The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, Text Revision (DSM-5-TR), published by the American Psychiatric Association, defines adult ADHD as a persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning or development. 
[1-2] For adults aged 17 years and older, the diagnostic threshold is reduced to at least five of nine symptoms in either the inattention domain (Criterion A1) or the hyperactivity-impulsivity domain (Criterion A2), persisting for at least six months, to a degree inconsistent with developmental level and negatively impacting social, academic, or occupational activities. 
[1][3-4] This adult-specific threshold reflects the recognition that symptom expression attenuates with age while remaining clinically significant. 
[2-3]

Volkow and Swanson, writing in The New England Journal of Medicine, provide a comprehensive overview of the DSM-5 criteria as applied to adults, including specific examples of how ADHD manifests across the lifespan. 
[2] The inattention domain encompasses symptoms such as frequent failure to attend to details, difficulty sustaining attention, seeming not to listen, failure to follow through on tasks, disorganization, avoidance of sustained mental effort, losing necessary items, distractibility, and forgetfulness in daily activities. 
[1][4] The hyperactivity-impulsivity domain includes fidgeting, leaving one's seat, restlessness, difficulty engaging quietly in leisure activities, being "on the go," excessive talking, blurting out answers, difficulty waiting one's turn, and interrupting others. 
[1][4]

The DSM-5-TR specifies three presentations: combined (both A1 and A2 met), predominantly inattentive (only A1 met), and predominantly hyperactive-impulsive (only A2 met), with the term "presentation" replacing "subtype" to reflect developmental instability of symptom clusters. 
[2-3] Additional criteria require that several symptoms were present before age 12 (Criterion B), symptoms manifest in two or more settings (Criterion C), clear functional impairment exists (Criterion D), and symptoms are not better explained by another mental disorder or psychotic condition (Criterion E). 
[1-2][5] Severity specifiers (mild, moderate, severe) and a partial remission specifier are also available. 
[1] Collateral information from informants who have observed the patient across settings is strongly recommended, as self-report alone is prone to recall bias and limited insight. 
[2][5-6]

ASRS v1.1 Screening in Brazil
The Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale v1.1 (ASRS v1.1), developed by the World Health Organization, is the most widely used screening instrument for adult ADHD globally and has been translated and culturally adapted for Brazilian Portuguese. 
[7-8] The American Academy of Family Physicians lists the ASRS v1.1 as the official WHO screening tool for adult ADHD. 
[5] In the original validation by Kessler et al. (2005), the 6-item screener demonstrated sensitivity of 68.7% and specificity of 99.5% in a US community sample. 
[8] In primary care settings, sensitivity approaches 1.0 with specificity of 0.71, a positive predictive value (PPV) of 0.52, and a negative predictive value (NPV) of 1.0. 
[9] The updated DSM-5 version described by Ustun et al. in JAMA Psychiatry achieved sensitivity above 90% using machine learning-optimized item selection. 
[6]

The cross-cultural psychometric analysis by Lewczuk et al. (2024), which included Brazil among 42 countries, demonstrated good reliability and validity with partial measurement invariance, confirming the instrument's applicability in the Brazilian context. 
[7] However, the ASRS v1.1 has important limitations: PPV can be as low as 11.5% in general population samples, and in psychiatric outpatient populations, 54.8% screened positive while only 11.9% had a formal diagnosis. 
[10-11] The optimization of the DSM-5 ASRS screener is illustrated by the area-under-the-curve analysis from Ustun et al. published in JAMA Psychiatry, which demonstrates how the number of screening questions relates to classification accuracy. 
[6]

For protocol implementation in the Brazilian supplementary health system, the ASRS v1.1 should serve as the initial screening step, with completion time averaging under one minute. 
[9] A positive screen must trigger a comprehensive clinical evaluation using DSM-5-TR criteria, structured clinical interview, and collateral information gathering, as the instrument is not sufficient for diagnosis. 
[5][7][10] In substance use disorder populations, the ASRS maintains high sensitivity (0.84–0.88) and NPV of 0.97, though specificity is moderate (0.66–0.67). 
[12]

Pre-Treatment ECG Indications for Stimulant Therapy
The cardiovascular safety of stimulant medications, including methylphenidate (metilfenidato) and lisdexamfetamine (lisdexanfetamina), is a central concern in protocol design. These agents produce mean increases in blood pressure of approximately 2–4 mm Hg and heart rate of 3–6 bpm. 
[13-14] The FDA labeling for lisdexamfetamine specifically cautions against use in patients with known structural cardiac abnormalities, cardiomyopathy, serious cardiac arrhythmia, or coronary artery disease. 
[14]

The guideline consensus, as articulated by the American Medical Society for Sports Medicine, the American Academy of Pediatrics, and NICE, supports a risk-based rather than universal ECG approach. 
[15-16] The American Academy of Pediatrics issued a policy statement that does not support routine ECG before stimulant initiation, contending there is no proof of increased sudden cardiac death rates in patients taking ADHD medications. 
[15] Pre-treatment ECG is indicated when any of the following cardiovascular risk factors are present: personal history of structural heart disease, cardiomyopathy, or serious arrhythmia; family history of sudden cardiac death, inherited arrhythmia syndromes, or cardiomyopathy; unexplained syncope or palpitations at rest; or abnormal findings on cardiovascular physical examination. 
[14-16] For adults without these risk factors, baseline blood pressure and heart rate measurement is sufficient. 
[15][17]

This risk-based logic applies to any stimulant molecule, not solely to metilfenidato or lisdexanfetamina, ensuring protocol durability if additional agents become available. The network meta-analysis by Farhat et al. (2025) in The Lancet Psychiatry confirmed that stimulants were not associated with larger hemodynamic increments compared to non-stimulants such as atomoxetine, reinforcing that cardiovascular monitoring principles are consistent across ADHD medication classes. 
[16] During ongoing therapy, Cortese, writing in The New England Journal of Medicine, recommends that if sustained resting tachycardia exceeding 120 bpm, arrhythmia, or systolic blood pressure above the 95th percentile occurs on two occasions, dose reduction and specialist referral are indicated. 
[17]

The long-term cardiovascular risk associated with cumulative ADHD medication use was examined by Zhang et al. in JAMA Psychiatry (2024), who found associations between increasing cumulative duration of methylphenidate and lisdexamfetamine use and incident cardiovascular disease, though the absolute risk remained low. 
[18]

For longstanding ADHD patients already stable on stimulant therapy, the pre-treatment ECG checklist does not apply. Neuropsychological Evaluation Indications
Formal neuropsychological evaluation is not universally required for adult ADHD diagnosis according to major guidelines. The American Academy of Family Physicians emphasizes that a combination of clinical evaluation, screening, diagnostic testing, and collateral information is often sufficient, particularly when there is a personal or family history of ADHD or a childhood diagnosis. 
[5] The European Psychiatric Association's Updated European Consensus Statement similarly supports clinical assessment as the primary diagnostic method, and the UK Adult ADHD Network (UKAAN) does not mandate neuropsychological testing as part of routine diagnosis. 
[19-20]

Neuropsychological evaluation is recommended in the following specific clinical scenarios: when corroborating collateral information is unavailable (no first-degree relative with ADHD, no childhood records, no observer reports); when confounding comorbid mental illness is suspected that may mimic or obscure ADHD symptoms; when malingering or invalid symptom presentation is suspected, particularly in medicolegal or disability assessment contexts; when the cognitive profile is complicated by other neuropsychiatric or medical conditions; and when objective documentation of cognitive impairment is required for disability determination or legal proceedings. 
[5][21-23] Nikolas et al. found that a combined approach using self and informant symptom ratings, positive family history, and a reaction time variability measure correctly classified 87% of cases, supporting the adjunctive value of neuropsychological testing in complex cases. 
[22]

When informant reports are unavailable, clinicians should employ alternative strategies including retrospective review of school, medical, and work records; structured clinical interviews reconstructing the symptom timeline; family history assessment; functional impairment documentation across settings; and validated self-report tools interpreted in clinical context. 
[2][5][24-25] The "or rule" recommended by Sibley (2021) integrates informant reports by considering symptoms endorsed by either the patient or an informant as present, provided they meet the impairment criterion. 
[23] The financial burden and limited accessibility of neuropsychological testing in Brazilian ambulatory settings should be considered, and its use should be reserved for cases where it is likely to impact clinical decision-making. 
[5]

Atomoxetine, a selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor, is the best-supported non-stimulant for adult ADHD, with meta-analytic effect sizes consistently favoring it over placebo (Hedge's g = −0.48, 95% CI −0.64 to −0.33).

ADHD with Comorbid Bipolar Disorder
In adults with ADHD and comorbid bipolar disorder (TAB), the central concern is mood destabilization. The expert consensus, articulated by Perugi and Vannucchi (2015) and Mucci et al. (2019), mandates that mood stabilization with appropriate mood stabilizers must precede the initiation of any ADHD pharmacotherapy. 
[29-30] Atomoxetine may be effective for ADHD symptoms in bipolar patients but carries a modestly increased risk of (hypo)manic switches when used without mood stabilizers. 
[29-30] When co-administered with mood stabilizers, this risk is reduced, and atomoxetine can be considered safe and effective. 
[29] Bupropion is generally considered to have a lower risk of inducing mania compared to other antidepressants, but evidence in the ADHD-bipolar population is limited. 
[28][31] The hierarchical approach is essential: stabilize mood first with lithium, valproate, or other mood stabilizers; then cautiously introduce atomoxetine (starting at 40 mg/day, titrating to 80–100 mg/day over 2–4 weeks) or bupropion (150–450 mg/day extended-release) with careful monitoring for manic or hypomanic symptoms. 
[29-31] Stimulants may be cautiously reintroduced only after sustained mood stabilization, with close monitoring. 
[29]

ADHD with Comorbid Substance Use Disorder
In adults with ADHD and comorbid substance use disorder (TUS), the primary concern is abuse potential of controlled stimulants. Risk stratification requires distinguishing active substance use (ongoing or within the past 3–6 months, confirmed by self-report, collateral information, or positive toxicology) from sustained remission (abstinence of at least 3–6 months with stable psychosocial functioning). 
[32-33] The American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry recommends a complete psychiatric evaluation including detailed drug and alcohol history, urine drug screening, and collateral information from a spouse, parent, or friend. 
[34]

For patients with active SUD, stimulant therapy is generally contraindicated, and atomoxetine is preferred due to its lack of abuse potential. 
[5][32][35] Atomoxetine reliably improves ADHD symptoms but does not consistently reduce substance craving or use. 
[35] For patients in sustained remission, stimulant therapy may be considered with robust risk mitigation: extended-release or prodrug formulations (lisdexamfetamine, controlled-release methylphenidate) are preferred for their lower abuse potential. 
[5][36-37] The American Academy of Addiction Psychiatry emphasizes risk mitigation measures including controlled substance agreements, prescription drug monitoring program (PDMP) review, random urine drug screening, pill counts, and increased visit frequency. 
[37] The overall cardiovascular risk associated with ADHD medications across age groups, including adults, remains low as demonstrated by the meta-analysis by Zhang et al. published in JAMA Network Open.

DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria and Late-Diagnosis Considerations
Core Criteria and Severity Specifiers
The American Psychiatric Association's DSM-5-TR defines Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) through five criteria (A through E) applicable across the lifespan. 
[1-3] Criterion A requires persistent deficits in all three subdomains of social communication and interaction: social-emotional reciprocity, nonverbal communicative behaviors, and developing/maintaining relationships. Criterion B requires at least two of four types of restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities: stereotyped/repetitive motor movements or speech, insistence on sameness or inflexible routines, highly restricted fixated interests abnormal in intensity, and hyper- or hyporeactivity to sensory input. 
[1-2] Criterion C stipulates that symptoms must be present in the early developmental period, though they may not become fully manifest until social demands exceed limited capacities or may be masked by learned strategies. Criterion D requires clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of current functioning. Criterion E specifies that disturbances are not better explained by intellectual disability or global developmental delay. 
[1][3]

The DSM-5-TR severity specifiers are independent of cognitive functioning and are based on the level of support required: Level 1 ("Requiring support") denotes noticeable impairments without supports in place; Level 2 ("Requiring substantial support") indicates marked deficits apparent even with supports; Level 3 ("Requiring very substantial support") reflects severe deficits markedly interfering with all areas of functioning. 
[2][4] Severity is specified separately for social communication and restricted/repetitive behaviors. The American Academy of Family Physicians emphasizes that the terms "high-functioning" and "low-functioning" have been eliminated, and severity is determined by adaptive function rather than cognitive ability. 
[4]

Late-Diagnosis Phenotypes and Camouflaging
Late diagnosis of ASD in adults is increasingly recognized, particularly among women and individuals with average or above-average intelligence. The DSM-5-TR explicitly acknowledges that manifestations may be masked by compensatory strategies, especially in individuals with higher cognitive and language abilities. 
[1] A recent study of 234 adults undergoing telehealth autism evaluation found that social-emotional reciprocity (OR = 5.21) and nonverbal communication (OR = 4.82) were the strongest predictors of ASD diagnosis, followed by relationship differences (OR = 3.63), need for routines (OR = 2.57), and repetitive behaviors (OR = 2.20), while intense interests and sensory processing differences showed limited predictive utility. 
[5] Sex assigned at birth did not meaningfully enhance diagnostic prediction beyond core symptom presentation, except for a modest interaction effect with nonverbal communication in individuals assigned female at birth. 
[5]

Camouflaging encompasses assimilation (adopting neurotypical behaviors), compensation (developing alternative strategies), and masking (concealing autistic traits). Empirical data using the Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire demonstrate that autistic females report higher camouflaging across all domains compared to males, and adulthood-diagnosed individuals exhibit elevated assimilation and compensation relative to those diagnosed in childhood. 
[6] Specific camouflaging behaviors include mimicking neurotypical communication styles, rehearsing social scripts, and consciously monitoring facial expressions and eye contact. 
[7] The "female autism phenotype" is characterized by restricted interests involving people or animals rather than objects, better superficial conversational skills, and subtle indicators such as excessive shyness, perfectionism, bossiness, or demanding extreme loyalty from friends. 
[7-8] These adaptations contribute to diagnostic overshadowing, where autistic traits are misattributed to anxiety, depression, personality disorders, or eating disorders. 
[9] A large study found that 77% of autistic females and 62% of autistic males received at least one psychiatric diagnosis, underscoring the frequency of misdiagnosis. 
[9] Qualitative research confirms that masking strategies contribute to delayed diagnoses and expose women to additional mental health risks including chronic exhaustion, identity confusion, and autistic burnout. 
[10-12]

Screening, Diagnostic Tools, and Psychiatrist's Role
The gold standard for adult ASD diagnosis remains multidisciplinary clinical consensus integrating developmental history, direct observation, and standardized instruments. 
[3][7] The Autism Spectrum Quotient (AQ) demonstrates sensitivity of approximately 0.77–0.79 and specificity of 0.29–0.77, with specificity notably reduced in clinical populations with psychiatric comorbidity. 
[13-14] The Ritvo Autism Asperger Diagnostic Scale-Revised (RAADS-R) shows sensitivity of 92.5–97% and specificity of 71–100% across validation studies, with the advantage of including sensory-motor symptoms aligned with DSM-5 criteria. 
[15-17] However, the RAADS-R's false positive rate reaches 55.6% in psychiatric populations. 
[18] Combining the AQ with the Empathy Quotient and Childhood Autism Spectrum Test-Relatives' Questionnaire yields a 98.3% likelihood of ASD diagnosis when all three screens are positive. 
[19] The ADOS-2 Module 4 achieves sensitivity of 91% and specificity of 76% in adult populations, though specificity drops in community mental health settings, particularly among adults with psychosis (30% false positive rate). 
[3][20] The ADI-R shows sensitivity of approximately 0.80 and specificity of 0.72 in adults without intellectual disability. 
[21-22] Critically, ADOS-2 scores below the diagnostic cutoff should not be used to rule out ASD in high-masking individuals, especially women. 
[7]

The psychiatrist's operational responsibilities at the diagnostic interface include heightened clinical suspicion in individuals with persistent social-communication difficulties, targeted inquiry into masking behaviors and developmental history, judicious use of screening tools (AQ, RAADS-R) to guide referral, and provision of a clear clinical summary to the multidisciplinary team. 
[3-4][7][23] The American Academy of Family Physicians notes that knowledgeable clinicians comfortable applying DSM-5-TR criteria can provide a preliminary diagnosis to expedite access to services, but formal diagnosis should involve comprehensive multidisciplinary assessment including neuropsychology, speech-language pathology, and cognitive/adaptive testing. 
[4] The psychiatrist should systematically evaluate for differential diagnoses including personality disorders, anxiety, depression, OCD, and ADHD, and ensure that the diagnostic process is person-centered and neurodiversity-affirming. 
[23-25]

Priority-Ordered Pharmacotherapy for Symptom Targets
Irritability and Antipsychotic Risk Profile
Pharmacotherapy in adults with ASD is directed at associated symptoms and comorbidities, as no medication is approved for core social-communication deficits. The evidence base is strongest for irritability and behavioral dysregulation. Risperidone and aripiprazole are FDA-approved for irritability in ASD, with standardized mean differences (SMD) of approximately 1.07–1.18 compared to placebo, consistent with a large clinical effect. 
[3][26-27] Risperidone should be initiated at 0.5 mg once or twice daily, titrated at intervals of at least two weeks to a target dose of 1–3 mg/day (mean modal dose in trials: 1.4–1.9 mg/day). 
[27-28] Aripiprazole is initiated at 2 mg once daily, titrated weekly to 5–15 mg/day with dose adjustments of up to 5 mg/day at intervals of no less than one week. 
[27][29-30] Lurasidone probably results in little to no difference in irritability compared to placebo. 
[27][31]

A class-level warning applies to all atypical antipsychotics in ASD: systematic reviews consistently report heightened risk of extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS), metabolic syndrome (weight gain, hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia), and sedation, with ASD patients demonstrating greater sensitivity to these effects than the general psychiatric population. 
[27][32-33] The Cochrane network meta-analysis found very low-certainty evidence for increased EPS (RR 2.36, 95% CI 1.22–4.59) and weight gain (RR 2.40, 95% CI 1.25–4.60) with atypical antipsychotics. 
[27] Antipsychotics should be reserved for severe irritability/aggression, used at the lowest effective dose, and monitored with baseline and periodic metabolic screening (weight, fasting glucose, lipid panel) and neurological assessment for EPS and akathisia. 
[27][32-33]

Comorbidity-Specific Pharmacotherapy
For ASD with comorbid anxiety, the Lurie Center for Autism guidelines recommend buspirone and mirtazapine as preferred agents due to better tolerability compared to SSRIs. 
[33] SSRIs should be initiated at one-third to one-half of the usual adult dose and titrated slowly: sertraline at 25 mg daily (target 50–200 mg/day), escitalopram at 5 mg daily (target 10–20 mg/day), fluoxetine at 10 mg daily (target 20–60 mg/day), and fluvoxamine at 25 mg daily (target 100–300 mg/day), with dose changes at intervals of one to two weeks. 
[33-34] SSRIs carry risk of behavioral activation, increased stereotypies, and agitation in ASD, necessitating the "start low, go slow" approach. 
[33-35] Mirtazapine is initiated at 15 mg once daily (evening), titrated in 15 mg increments every one to two weeks to a maximum of 45 mg/day, with monitoring for sedation and weight gain. 
[33][36-37] A pilot RCT of mirtazapine for anxiety in ASD youth showed a within-group effect size of 1.76 on the Pediatric Anxiety Rating Scale, with 47% of mirtazapine-treated participants rated "much improved" or "very much improved" versus 20% on placebo. 
[37]

For ASD with comorbid ADHD, alpha-2 adrenergic agonists are recommended as first-line agents. Extended-release guanfacine is initiated at 1 mg once daily, titrated weekly to 1–4 mg/day (modal dose in trials: 3 mg/day), with a 43.6% decline in hyperactivity scores versus 13.2% for placebo (effect size = 1.67) and a 50% positive response rate versus 9.4% for placebo. 
[38-39] Atomoxetine is initiated at 40 mg once daily, increased to 80 mg after a minimum of three days, with a maximum of 100 mg/day, showing an effect size of approximately 0.5 for overall ADHD symptoms. 
[3][33][40] Methylphenidate demonstrates a moderate effect size (SMD ≈ 0.6) for hyperactivity but carries increased risk of behavioral activation, restlessness, and stereotypies in ASD. 
[3][34][41] Stimulants should be used cautiously, starting at low doses and monitoring for increased stereotypies, irritability, and anxiety. 
[3][33]

For ASD with comorbid depression, the Lurie Center guidelines recommend duloxetine, mirtazapine, bupropion, and vortioxetine ahead of SSRIs. 
[33] Simon et al. published in JAMA provide comprehensive guidance on first-line pharmacotherapy for depression in the general adult population, noting that escitalopram and sertraline rank in the top half for both efficacy and acceptability, and that antidepressant medication should usually be initiated at one-third to one-half of the usual dose. 
[42]

Autistic Burnout Versus Major Depression
Autistic burnout is a clinically significant syndrome characterized by profound exhaustion, loss of previously acquired functional skills, cognitive difficulties, and increased expression of core autistic traits, precipitated by chronic masking, sensory/social overload, and sustained demands in unaccommodating environments. 
[45-46] A systematic review synthesizing 48 studies (approximately 4,000 autistic participants) found that burnout is chronic with intermittent crises, and that sensory overwhelm, camouflaging, stigma, and everyday life challenges are prominent contributors. 
[45] Recovery is facilitated by rest, solitude, sensory relief, and supportive environments rather than by behavioral activation. 
[45-46]

Key differentiating features from major depressive disorder (MDD) include etiology (burnout is precipitated by masking and environmental stress, not biopsychosocial factors broadly), symptom profile (burnout manifests as exhaustion and loss of skills with increased autistic traits rather than pervasive low mood and anhedonia), temporal pattern (burnout is chronic/intermittent following periods of intense masking, while depression is persistent and less situational), and response to intervention (burnout responds to demand reduction and sensory relief, while depression responds to activation and antidepressants). 
[46-47] A Delphi consensus led by autistic adults explicitly warns that psychological treatments for depression, particularly behavioral activation, could potentially worsen autistic burnout if they increase demands without addressing sensory and environmental needs. 
[46]

For treatment, when autistic burnout predominates, interventions should prioritize environmental modification, reduction of demands, and adapted psychotherapeutic approaches. A network meta-analysis found that mindfulness-based interventions ranked highest for anxiety reduction (SMD = −0.84, SUCRA = 91.4%) and CBT ranked highest for depression reduction (SMD = −0.77, SUCRA = 90.1%) in ASD populations. 
[48] Both CBT and mindfulness-based stress reduction showed sustained effects at three-month follow-up in adults with ASD. 
[49] The ADEPT feasibility RCT demonstrated that adapted low-intensity CBT informed by behavioral activation was feasible and well-received in autistic adults with depression, with 86% attending the pre-defined treatment dose. 
[50-51] However, psychological therapies in primary care yield a recovery rate of only 32.9% in autistic adults, substantially below the 50% national target in the UK, underscoring the need for autism-specific adaptations. 
[52]

Simon et al. published in JAMA provide a framework for severity-based initial treatment selection for depression in the general population, which can inform clinical decision-making when MDD is confirmed in an autistic adult. 
[42]

Integrated Clinical Recommendations
For newly diagnosed adults, the psychiatrist should implement a structured pathway beginning with heightened clinical suspicion when patients present with longstanding social-communication difficulties, atypical psychiatric symptoms, or treatment-resistant anxiety/depression. Screening with the AQ (cutoff ≥26 for referral) and/or RAADS-R (cutoff ≥65 in the original validation, ≥81 in the German validation) should guide referral decisions. 
[13][15][17] Referral for comprehensive multidisciplinary assessment should include ADOS-2 Module 4, developmental history (ADI-R or structured interview with informant), cognitive testing (Wechsler scales), adaptive behavior assessment (Vineland), and speech-language evaluation. 
[3-4][53] The psychiatrist should provide a detailed clinical summary to the team, noting observed masking behaviors, and should advocate for longer assessments and naturalistic observation in high-masking individuals. 
[7][25]

For adults with longstanding ASD diagnoses, the clinical pathway focuses on comorbidity management following the priority order: irritability/behavioral dysregulation (risperidone or aripiprazole as first-line, with guanfacine for milder cases), anxiety (buspirone or mirtazapine preferred over SSRIs), repetitive behaviors (limited pharmacological options; individualize based on comorbid OCD/anxiety), and attention/executive function (guanfacine or atomoxetine preferred over stimulants). 
[3][26][32-33] Across all agents, the "start low, go slow" principle is paramount, with dose increments at intervals of one to two weeks and regular monitoring for metabolic parameters (weight, fasting glucose, lipids at baseline and every 3–6 months for antipsychotics), neurological symptoms (EPS, akathisia), and behavioral changes (activation, stereotypies). 
[27][32-33] The class-level warning for heightened EPS and metabolic sensitivity in ASD applies to all atypical antipsychotics regardless of agent chosen, and clinicians should use the lowest effective dose with periodic reassessment of continued need. 
[27][32-33] All pharmacotherapy should be individualized within an interdisciplinary framework, with clear treatment targets and regular outcome monitoring to optimize care for this clinically complex population.

DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria for Borderline Personality Disorder
The American Psychiatric Association defines borderline personality disorder (BPD) in the DSM-5-TR as a pervasive pattern of instability in interpersonal relationships, self-image, and affects, accompanied by marked impulsivity, beginning by early adulthood and present across a variety of contexts. 
[1-2] The diagnostic model is polythetic: a formal diagnosis requires the presence of at least five of nine specified criteria. These criteria encompass frantic efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment; a pattern of unstable and intense interpersonal relationships characterized by alternating idealization and devaluation; identity disturbance with markedly and persistently unstable self-image or sense of self; impulsivity in at least two areas that are potentially self-damaging (excluding suicidal or self-mutilating behavior); recurrent suicidal behavior, gestures, threats, or self-mutilating behavior; affective instability due to marked reactivity of mood (intense episodic dysphoria, irritability, or anxiety usually lasting a few hours and rarely more than a few days); chronic feelings of emptiness; inappropriate, intense anger or difficulty controlling anger; and transient, stress-related paranoid ideation or severe dissociative symptoms. 
[1-3]

Sharp et al. published a comprehensive summary of these criteria in The New England Journal of Medicine, which is reproduced below as a reference for clinical operationalization. 
[2]

Beyond meeting at least five of nine criteria, the DSM-5-TR requires that symptoms be relatively inflexible and pervasive across multiple contexts, result in significant distress or functional impairment, be stable across time with onset traceable to adolescence or early adulthood, and not be better explained by another mental disorder or attributable to the physiological effects of a substance or medical condition. 
[2][4] The DSM-5-TR Section III alternative model for personality disorders (AMPD) provides a dimensional complement, requiring moderate or greater impairment in at least two of four personality functioning domains (identity, self-direction, empathy, intimacy) plus four or more of seven pathological traits (emotional lability, anxiousness, separation insecurity, depressivity, impulsivity, risk-taking, hostility), with at least one being impulsivity, risk-taking, or hostility. 
[3-4] While the categorical model remains the standard for clinical diagnosis, the AMPD may be useful for complex or research-oriented assessments.

Regarding adolescent applicability, the DSM-5-TR does not prohibit diagnosis before age 18 but recommends caution, stipulating that features must be present for at least one year and not attributable to normal developmental variation. 
[1][5] Evidence supports the validity and reliability of BPD diagnosis in adolescents from age 12 onward, with community prevalence slightly exceeding adult rates and clinical settings reporting rates up to 35.6%. 
[4-6] For the protocol's adolescent decision point, this is addressed in the final section below.

Differentiating NSSI from Suicidal Behavior at Triage
Clinical Features and Intent Assessment
The primary clinical distinction between non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI) and suicidal behavior is the presence or absence of intent to die. The DSM-5-TR defines NSSI as deliberate, self-inflicted damage to body tissue without suicidal intent and not culturally sanctioned, performed most often to relieve internal distress, regulate affect, or self-punish. 
[1] Suicidal behavior, by contrast, is defined by an explicit or inferred wish to die, which may be accompanied by a plan, preparation, or high-lethality method. 
[7] This distinction is structurally critical for outpatient triage: NSSI without suicidal intent remains within the BPD treatment module and informs treatment intensity, while suicidal ideation with intent or plan routes to crisis triage (Gate P0).

The Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) is the most widely endorsed instrument for operationalizing this distinction. The American Psychiatric Association, the Department of Veterans Affairs, and the Joint Commission all recommend the C-SSRS for suicide risk screening and triage. 
[8-10] The brief C-SSRS consists of six cascading questions assessing wish to be dead, suicidal thoughts, suicidal thoughts with method, suicidal intent without plan, suicidal intent with plan, and suicidal behavior. 
[11] A positive response to any degree of suicidal thinking in the past month or any history of suicide attempt predicts suicidality with a positive likelihood ratio greater than 10. 
[12] The Self-Injurious Thoughts and Behaviors Interview–Revised (SITBI-R) provides complementary structured assessment of NSSI and suicidal behaviors, with strong convergent validity with the C-SSRS. 
[13] Both instruments should be administered without modification of question wording, as the Joint Commission warns that even small changes can affect accuracy. 
[10]

Collateral information from family or caregivers is essential, as fewer than one-third of patients with suicidal behaviors express their intent to clinicians. 
[7] The Collaborative Longitudinal Study of Personality Disorders demonstrated over 10 years of follow-up that identity disturbance, frantic efforts to avoid abandonment, and chronic feelings of emptiness were the most robust independent predictors of suicide attempts in BPD, even after controlling for all other criteria. 
[14] Yen et al. published survival analysis data illustrating the time to first suicide attempt in BPD, which underscores the longitudinal risk trajectory in this population. 
[14]

Hopelessness and worthlessness are more strongly associated with suicidal behavior than with NSSI alone, while violent thoughts toward others are more common in patients with NSSI without suicide attempts. 
[15] These features should be explicitly assessed at triage to inform routing decisions.

Escalation Indicators and Risk Stratification
Not all NSSI in BPD is low-risk. Escalating patterns of NSSI—defined by increased frequency (e.g., weekly or daily episodes), greater medical severity (deeper wounds, need for suturing or hospitalization), use of multiple methods, and evidence of pain habituation or reduced pain sensitivity—are associated with heightened risk for transition to suicidal behavior. 
[16-17] Hepp et al. demonstrated in a sample of 1,227 individuals with recent NSSI that higher frequency, greater number of methods, increased severity, need for medical attention, and reductions in pain over time were all significantly associated with greater suicidal behavior and more suicide attempts, consistent with the interpersonal theory of suicide's concept of acquired capability. 
[16] Knorr et al. found that the number of lifetime NSSI methods, NSSI frequency, depressive symptoms, and self-disclosure of suicidal ideation most parsimoniously differentiated individuals along the continuum from no suicidal history to suicide attempt history. 
[17]

For protocol operationalization, escalating NSSI should trigger treatment intensification (referral to or intensification of DBT, increased monitoring frequency to weekly or every 2–3 days, functional behavioral analysis) even in the absence of explicit suicidal intent. 
[4][18] The clinical staging model described in The Lancet recommends matching treatment intensity to severity, with generalist interventions for early-stage presentations and intensive specialist psychosocial treatment for persistent pathology or escalating self-harm. 
[4]

DBT as First-Line Psychotherapy for BPD
Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) is the most thoroughly studied and replicated evidence-based psychotherapy for BPD. The most recent Cochrane review (75 RCTs, 4,507 participants) found that psychotherapy, predominantly DBT, reduced BPD symptom severity compared to treatment-as-usual (TAU) with a standardized mean difference (SMD) of −0.52 (95% CI −0.70 to −0.33), exceeding the minimum clinically relevant difference of −3.0 points on the Zanarini Rating Scale for BPD. 
[19-20] Subgroup analyses demonstrated that DBT specifically reduced BPD severity (SMD −0.60), self-harm (SMD −0.28), and improved psychosocial functioning (SMD −0.36) compared to TAU. 
[19-20] The focused meta-analysis by Stoffers-Winterling et al. reported moderate effect sizes for DBT in reducing self-harm (SMD −0.54) and improving psychosocial functioning (SMD −0.51). 
[21]

Standard DBT comprises four components: weekly individual therapy (1 hour), weekly group skills training (1.5–2.5 hours), between-session telephone coaching as needed, and a weekly therapist consultation team meeting (1–2 hours). 
[3][8][22] The component analysis by Linehan et al. demonstrated that the full DBT package (individual therapy plus group skills training) is superior to partial versions in reducing suicide attempts, NSSI episodes, and acute service utilization. 
[22] DBT is the only treatment with sufficient replication to be considered evidence-based for BPD. 
[22] Retention is notably high, with dropout rates of approximately 27%, which is favorable compared to other psychotherapies for this population. 
[22-23]

The most recent RCT by Brodsky et al. (2025) compared six months of DBT to SSRI with clinical management in 84 individuals with BPD and found significantly fewer suicide-related events and NSSI behaviors in the DBT arm during the treatment phase, though the reduction in risk for suicide attempts was not statistically significant in survival analysis. 
[24] The Department of Veterans Affairs guideline notes that, despite a growing body of studies, pooled estimates show no significant effect of DBT on suicide deaths or attempts specifically, though DBT is effective in reducing the frequency and medical severity of NSSI. 
[8]

Referral criteria for DBT should include formal BPD diagnosis (≥5/9 DSM-5-TR criteria), presence of NSSI or suicidality, escalating self-harm patterns, and psychosocial impairment sufficient to warrant intensive outpatient intervention. 
[4][20][23]

Adjunctive Pharmacotherapy: Acute and Long-Term
Acute Crisis and Waitlist Pharmacotherapy
No medication is approved by the US Food and Drug Administration or licensed in the UK for BPD treatment. 
[3][23] The UK National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guideline, as cited in JAMA, explicitly recommends against pharmacotherapy for any core BPD symptom and advises that, in crisis, drug treatment should use a single agent at the minimum effective dose for no longer than one week. 
[3][25] For acute crises, NICE recommends sedative antihistamines (e.g., promethazine) or low-potency antipsychotics (e.g., quetiapine), but not benzodiazepines. 
[3] For severe insomnia, short-term use of Z-drugs (zolpidem, eszopiclone) at the lowest dose for no longer than four weeks may be considered. 
[3]

When symptom-targeted pharmacotherapy is required during DBT waitlist periods or acute destabilization, the most recent network meta-analysis (Gerolymos et al., 2026; 35 RCTs, 2,551 participants) provides the following evidence: topiramate (200–250 mg/day for 8–10 weeks), lamotrigine (50–200 mg/day for 8 weeks), and aripiprazole (15 mg/day for 8 weeks) most effectively reduced hostility, aggressiveness, and anger, with high, moderate, and moderate levels of evidence, respectively. 
[26] Carbamazepine (200–1,200 mg/day for 6 weeks) showed low-certainty evidence for impulsivity, and asenapine (5–10 mg/day for 12 weeks) showed very low-certainty evidence for emotional dysregulation. 
[26] However, the Cochrane review (2022; 46 RCTs, 2,769 participants) found that, compared with placebo, no medication class consistently improved primary outcomes such as overall BPD symptom severity, self-harm, suicide-related outcomes, or psychosocial functioning, and the certainty of evidence was very low. 
[27-28]

For comorbid major depressive disorder or severe anxiety, SSRIs (escitalopram, sertraline, fluoxetine) may be prescribed per comorbidity-specific guidelines, but not for BPD symptoms per se. 
[3][29] Reassessment should occur within 3–7 days of medication initiation, with weekly follow-up until discontinuation and crisis resolution. 
[3-4]

Limitations and Polypharmacy Avoidance
The protocol must explicitly state that pharmacotherapy does not treat BPD as a primary target; it addresses symptom dimensions only. No medication alters the core pathology or long-term trajectory of BPD. 
[3][25][27] Polypharmacy is a frequent and problematic phenomenon: up to 80% of BPD patients are prescribed three or more psychotropic medications, often without demonstrable benefit. 
[30] Polypharmacy increases the risk of adverse effects, drug interactions, and medication noncompliance, and may undermine the therapeutic alliance. 
[23][30] Medications with unclear benefit should be discontinued before initiating new agents, and the use of controlled substances or medications with overdose risk should be avoided. 
[29-30] Olanzapine is associated with significant metabolic side effects, weight gain, sedation, and a possible increase in self-harming behaviors. 
[4][28] Lamotrigine, despite initial promise, failed to show benefit in a large, methodologically robust 12-month trial. 
[4] All medication decisions should be made in the context of ongoing clinical review, prioritizing safety and minimizing unnecessary pharmacological exposure. 
[3-4][27]

Adolescent Alert and Monitoring Framework
For late adolescent (16–17) presentations meeting the BPD symptom threshold, the protocol requires a three-branch decision point. Referral to specialized adolescent services is indicated when the patient exhibits persistent, pervasive BPD symptoms (≥5/9 criteria for ≥1 year), significant functional impairment, escalating self-harm, or complex comorbidities. 
[4-6] A provisional diagnosis with generalist outpatient management (psychoeducation, General Psychiatric Management for Adolescents [GPM-A], family support) is appropriate when symptoms meet threshold but duration is less than one year, impairment is mild to moderate, and there is no immediate risk. 
[4][31] The MOBY trial in JAMA Psychiatry demonstrated that general youth mental health service models combined with befriending produced clinically modest improvements, though specialist models were superior for rapid symptom change. 
[32] Chanen et al. published data on interpersonal and social adjustment outcomes across treatment arms, illustrating the comparative trajectories of early intervention approaches.

DSM-5-TR Diagnostic Criteria and Severity Classification
Anorexia Nervosa Criteria and Severity
The American Psychiatric Association defines Anorexia Nervosa (AN) in the DSM-5-TR by three essential criteria: (A) restriction of energy intake relative to requirements, leading to a significantly low body weight in the context of age, sex, developmental trajectory, and physical health; (B) intense fear of gaining weight or of becoming fat, or persistent behavior that interferes with weight gain, even though at a significantly low weight; and (C) disturbance in the way in which one's body weight or shape is experienced, undue influence of body weight or shape on self-evaluation, or persistent lack of recognition of the seriousness of the current low body weight. 
[1-3] For adults, a BMI less than 18.5 kg/m² is generally considered below the minimally normal range, with a BMI less than 17.0 kg/m² indicating moderate or severe thinness. 
[3-4]

The DSM-5-TR specifies two subtypes: the restricting type (F50.01), in which weight loss is accomplished primarily through dieting, fasting, and/or excessive exercise without recurrent binge-eating or purging during the last three months; and the binge-eating/purging type (F50.02), in which the individual has engaged in recurrent episodes of binge-eating or purging behavior during the last three months. 
[1-2] Remission specifiers include partial remission (Criterion A no longer met but B or C persists) and full remission (none of the criteria met for a sustained period). 
[1][5]

Severity in adults is classified by current BMI: mild (BMI ≥17 kg/m²), moderate (BMI 16–16.99 kg/m²), severe (BMI 15–15.99 kg/m²), and extreme (BMI <15 kg/m²). For children and adolescents, corresponding BMI percentiles should be used. The American Psychiatric Association notes that severity may be increased to reflect clinical symptoms, degree of functional disability, and need for supervision. 
[1][3-5]

Mitchell and Peterson published a comprehensive evaluation framework in The New England Journal of Medicine that details the recommended workup for patients with suspected AN, including physical examination findings, laboratory assessments, and clinical indicators stratified by severity. 
[3]

Bulimia Nervosa and Binge-Eating Disorder
Bulimia Nervosa (BN) is characterized by recurrent episodes of binge eating (eating an amount of food definitely larger than most individuals would eat under similar circumstances within a discrete period, accompanied by a sense of lack of control), followed by recurrent inappropriate compensatory behaviors (self-induced vomiting, misuse of laxatives, diuretics, fasting, or excessive exercise). These behaviors must occur at least once per week for three months, self-evaluation must be unduly influenced by body shape and weight, and the disturbance must not occur exclusively during episodes of AN. 
[1][5-6] Severity is based on the frequency of inappropriate compensatory behaviors per week: mild (1–3), moderate (4–7), severe (8–13), and extreme (≥14). 
[1][5]

Binge-Eating Disorder (BED) requires recurrent binge-eating episodes at least once per week for three months, associated with marked distress and at least three of the following: eating much more rapidly than normal, eating until uncomfortably full, eating large amounts when not physically hungry, eating alone because of embarrassment, and feeling disgusted, depressed, or guilty afterward. Critically, BED is distinguished from BN by the absence of regular inappropriate compensatory behaviors. 
[1][5][7] Severity follows the same frequency-based schema as BN: mild (1–3 binge episodes/week), moderate (4–7), severe (8–13), and extreme (≥14). 
[1][5]

The US Preventive Services Task Force published a clinician summary outlining the screening approach for eating disorders in adolescents and adults, which provides a practical framework for primary care identification. 
[8]

Clinical Parameters and Admission Thresholds
Medical Admission Thresholds
Inpatient admission for AN is indicated when acute medical instability is present. The American Psychiatric Association (APA) Practice Guideline recommends comprehensive assessment including vital signs (temperature, resting heart rate, blood pressure, orthostatic changes), BMI, laboratory evaluation (complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel with electrolytes, liver enzymes, renal function), and ECG for patients with restrictive eating disorders or severe purging. 
[9-10] Clinical consensus and the APA guideline indicate that a BMI ≤15 kg/m² is a critical threshold for hospitalization in adults, especially when accompanied by hemodynamic compromise. 
[3][7][9]

The NICE/MARSIPAN framework, used in the United Kingdom, provides the most operationalizable criteria for the ambulatory-to-inpatient threshold decision. High-risk patients requiring urgent admission include those with BMI <14 kg/m², heart rate <40–50 bpm, systolic blood pressure <80–90 mmHg, temperature <35.5°C, QTc >450 ms, hypokalemia <3.0 mmol/L, hypophosphatemia <0.5 mmol/L, and significant postural changes (orthostatic tachycardia >20 bpm increase or systolic blood pressure drop >20 mmHg). 
[4] The MARSIPAN framework also includes the SUSS (Sit-Up Squat Stand) test as a functional assessment of physical frailty. 
[4]

Adolescent-Specific Thresholds
For adolescents, absolute BMI values are inappropriate for admission decisions due to variability in growth trajectories. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends using age- and sex-adjusted BMI percentiles from the CDC Growth Charts. 
[6][11] A BMI below the 5th percentile for age and sex is a red flag for undernutrition and potential admission, while a BMI ≤75% of median BMI for age, sex, and height constitutes a red flag for acute medical stabilization. 
[6][12] Clinicians should also flag any rapid decline crossing two or more major percentile lines as indicative of acute weight loss requiring immediate evaluation. 
[11] Vital sign thresholds and laboratory values should be interpreted using age-adjusted norms, as bradycardia and hypotension thresholds may differ during adolescence. 
[6][13] Arrested growth and development, or failure to respond to outpatient treatment, are additional red flags specific to this population. 
[6]

Involuntary Admission Under Brazilian Law
In Brazil, two distinct admission pathways exist for patients with severe AN. General medical admission is triggered by acute medical instability (hemodynamic compromise, severe electrolyte disturbance, ECG abnormalities, BMI below critical threshold, or refeeding syndrome risk) and does not require psychiatric involuntary commitment criteria—medical urgency is sufficient. 
[4][7][9]

Psychiatric involuntary admission (internação involuntária) under Lei 10.216/2001 applies when the patient refuses necessary treatment and imminent medical risk is present. The Conselho Federal de Medicina (CFM), through Resolução nº 2.057/2013, operationalizes these requirements. The attending physician must provide a detailed written justification documenting the clinical diagnosis (DSM-5-TR), reasons for hospitalization, and therapeutic objectives. Within 72 hours, the physician must notify the local Ministério Público (Public Prosecutor's Office), providing a copy of the justification and relevant clinical information. 
[14-15] The patient and/or legal representative must be informed of the reasons for admission and their rights, including the right to appeal. Regular reassessment of the need for continued hospitalization is mandatory, with discharge required as soon as criteria are no longer met. 
[14-15] For AN specifically, thresholds for compulsory admission include BMI ≤15 kg/m², profound hypotension, severe electrolyte abnormalities, arrhythmias or severe bradycardia (<40 bpm), and suicide risk, when the patient refuses necessary care. 
[3][16-18]

A standardized documentation checklist for involuntary admission should include: patient identification; DSM-5-TR diagnosis with severity specifier and clinical parameters (BMI, labs, vitals); justification for admission describing acute risk and treatment refusal; therapeutic objectives; legal basis citing Lei 10.216/2001 and CFM Resolução nº 2.057/2013; confirmation that patient rights were communicated; date and method of Ministério Público notification; and schedule for regular reassessment and discharge planning. 
[14-15]

Medical Complications and Inpatient Monitoring
Anorexia nervosa carries the highest mortality rate among psychiatric conditions, with approximately one-third of deaths attributed to cardiovascular complications. 
[19] The hypometabolic state leads to decreased cardiac muscle mass, bradycardia, hypotension, prolonged QTc interval, and risk of ventricular arrhythmias and sudden cardiac death. 
[6][19-20] Hematological alterations and symptoms of medical instability occur in approximately 1 out of 5 youths with AN requiring hospitalization, with nearly 40% needing repeated hospitalizations. 
[19]

Hypokalemia, resulting from dietary restriction, vomiting, and laxative abuse, increases arrhythmia risk and requires prompt correction with oral or intravenous potassium replacement, guided by serum levels and ECG monitoring. 
[3][6][20] Hypophosphatemia is the hallmark of refeeding syndrome and can precipitate respiratory failure, hemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, seizures, and cardiac dysfunction. 
[6][21] The incidence of refeeding hypophosphatemia in hospitalized adolescents with AN is approximately 14%. 
[6]

Refeeding syndrome prevention and management require a protocolized approach. The American Society for Parenteral and Enteral Nutrition (ASPEN) recommends initiating thiamine supplementation at 100 mg orally or intravenously daily (up to 200 mg in severe cases; 2 mg/kg up to 100–200 mg/day in adolescents) before feeding commences, continuing for at least 5–7 days. 
[22] Electrolytes (potassium, phosphate, magnesium) should be checked before initiation of nutrition and monitored every 12 hours for the first 3 days in high-risk patients. 
[22] Prophylactic electrolyte supplementation targets include potassium 2–4 mmol/kg/day, phosphate 0.3–0.6 mmol/kg/day, and magnesium 0.2–0.4 mmol/kg/day. 
[22-23] A complete oral multivitamin should be added daily for at least 10 days. 
[22] Sodium intake should be limited to <1 mmol/kg/day and fluid to <20 mL/kg/day in the early refeeding phase. 
[23]

Initial caloric prescription for adults should be 1,500–2,000 kcal/day, with gradual increases of approximately 33% of goal every 1–2 days. 
[3][7][22] For mildly or moderately malnourished adolescents, recent evidence supports initial prescriptions of 2,200–2,600 kcal/day, achieving weight gain of approximately 1.4–2 kg per week. 
[6][24-25] For severely malnourished adolescents (<70% median BMI), a more cautious approach starting at lower caloric targets is warranted. 
[6] If electrolyte levels drop precipitously, ASPEN recommends reducing caloric intake by 50% and advancing by approximately 33% of goal every 1–2 days. 
[22]

Garber et al. published a multicenter randomized clinical trial in JAMA Pediatrics comparing higher-calorie refeeding (HCR) versus lower-calorie refeeding (LCR) in adolescents with AN, demonstrating that HCR achieved faster heart rate stabilization and greater weight gain without increased refeeding complications when electrolyte monitoring was rigorous. 
[24]

Ambulatory Monitoring and Refeeding Protocol
The APA and the American Academy of Family Physicians recommend that ambulatory monitoring of eating disorders include weight, BMI, and vital signs (resting and orthostatic) at every visit, with visits occurring weekly to biweekly during the acute phase and monthly as stability is achieved. 
[9-10][26] Laboratory tests (CBC, electrolytes, liver and renal function) should be obtained at baseline and repeated every 1–4 weeks depending on clinical risk. 
[10][26] ECG should be obtained at baseline and repeated if clinical status changes or laboratory abnormalities are detected. 
[7][9-10] Behavioral assessment for suicidality, acute food refusal, and failure of outpatient treatment should occur at every visit. 
[7][26]

Red-flag values requiring immediate referral or admission include: BMI ≤15 kg/m² in adults (≤75% median BMI or <5th percentile in adolescents); resting heart rate <40 bpm; systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg; orthostatic changes (Δ HR >20 bpm or Δ SBP >20 mmHg); temperature <35.5°C; potassium <3.0 mEq/L; phosphate <0.8 mmol/L; magnesium <1.5 mg/dL; QTc >450 ms in males or >470 ms in females; and acute behavioral crises including food refusal, uncontrollable purging, or suicidality. 
[7][9-10][12][26]

During the ambulatory-to-inpatient transition, the clinician must initiate refeeding syndrome prevention before transfer. This includes starting thiamine 100–200 mg daily orally, checking baseline electrolytes, initiating oral phosphate, potassium, and magnesium supplementation if levels are low, and beginning cautious caloric intake at 1,500 kcal/day with daily electrolyte monitoring. 
[21-23] If severe or refractory electrolyte abnormalities develop, nutrition support should be paused and urgent inpatient transfer arranged. 
[22]

The APA recommends that all patients with eating disorders have a documented, comprehensive, culturally appropriate, and person-centered treatment plan incorporating medical, psychiatric, psychological, and nutritional expertise via a coordinated multidisciplinary team. 
[9] The medical clinician is responsible for physical assessment, monitoring, and management of complications; the psychiatrist for psychiatric evaluation, psychotherapy (cognitive behavioral therapy as first-line for BN and BED; family-based therapy for adolescents), pharmacotherapy (fluoxetine 60 mg/day FDA-approved for BN; lisdexamfetamine FDA-approved for BED), and legal compliance; and the dietitian for nutritional assessment, individualized meal planning, caloric prescription, and supervision of nutritional rehabilitation. 
[7-9][26] During transitions of care, the team must ensure seamless communication, transfer of medical and psychiatric information, and continuity of nutritional therapy, with explicit escalation criteria documented in the treatment plan. 
[7][9-10]

Drug Interaction Monitoring Protocol
The four drug combinations most frequently encountered in ambulatory psychiatry—lithium combined with NSAIDs, valproate combined with lamotrigine, clozapine combined with benzodiazepines, and SSRIs combined with tramadol—each carry distinct mechanisms of harm, clinical signatures, and timeframes for toxicity that demand both acute recognition protocols and structured long-term surveillance. The protocol must operate on two tiers: an emergency response layer for immediate clinical deterioration and a scheduled monitoring layer for detecting subclinical harm before it becomes life-threatening.

Acute Recognition and Emergency Response
The interaction between lithium and NSAIDs is driven by NSAID-mediated reduction in renal blood flow, which decreases lithium clearance and elevates serum concentrations. FDA-approved labeling explicitly warns that NSAIDs decrease renal blood flow, resulting in decreased renal clearance and increased serum lithium concentrations, and recommends more frequent serum lithium concentration monitoring with dose reduction as needed. 
[1-3] A retrospective analysis of 501 psychiatric inpatients confirmed that NSAIDs independently increase lithium levels, with the number of potentially interacting drugs significantly associated with rising serum lithium. 
[4] Toxicity typically develops within 1 to 7 days of NSAID initiation, presenting as gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea), neuromuscular signs (tremor, ataxia), and neuropsychiatric deterioration (confusion, dysarthria, lethargy), progressing to seizures, coma, or cardiac arrhythmias in severe cases. 
[5-7] Emergency management requires immediate discontinuation of both the NSAID and lithium, urgent serum lithium measurement, renal function and electrolyte assessment, intravenous hydration, and cardiac monitoring. Hemodialysis is indicated when serum lithium exceeds 2.5 mEq/L or when severe neurological symptoms are present. 
[1-3]

Valproate inhibits lamotrigine glucuronidation, increasing lamotrigine's elimination half-life from 26 to 70 hours (a 165% increase), which substantially elevates the risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) and toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN). 
[8] The FDA labeling for lamotrigine mandates that when co-administered with valproate, lamotrigine must be initiated at half the usual dose and titrated no faster than every two weeks. 
[9] Smith et al. in the New England Journal of Medicine note that rash occurs in approximately 3.5% of patients on lamotrigine, with severe reactions especially likely when combined with valproate. 
[10] The critical window for SJS/TEN is the first 2 to 8 weeks of combined therapy, though onset can occur as early as 5 to 10 days with rapid titration. The acute clinical trigger is any new rash, particularly if accompanied by mucosal involvement, fever, or lymphadenopathy. The interaction between valproate and lamotrigine and the required dose adjustments are detailed in Table 4 from Smith, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, which summarizes first-line antiseizure medications including their interactions, adverse effects, and dosing considerations relevant to this combination.

Emergency management of suspected SJS/TEN requires immediate discontinuation of both lamotrigine and valproate, hospital admission, dermatology consultation, and supportive care including fluid management, wound care, and infection prevention. 
[8-10]

The combination of clozapine and benzodiazepines increases the risk of cardiorespiratory collapse, aspiration pneumonia, and delirium, particularly during the first week of clozapine titration. A 2025 systematic review by Quiles, de Leon, and Verdoux recommends that benzodiazepines should be avoided the week before clozapine initiation and during the first week of titration; if benzodiazepines are maintained, clozapine should be very slowly titrated with close monitoring for pneumonia symptoms for at least two weeks and daily delirium assessment. 
[11] The FDA clozapine label warns of concomitant CNS depression risks. 
[12] Acute signs include excessive sedation, hypotension, bradycardia, respiratory depression, and cardiorespiratory arrest, with the highest risk during the first two weeks of combined therapy. 
[11][13]

SSRIs combined with tramadol precipitate serotonin syndrome through dual serotonergic mechanisms: both agents increase synaptic serotonin, and SSRIs inhibit tramadol metabolism via CYP2D6, further elevating serotonin levels. 
[14-15] The FDA tramadol label warns that serotonin syndrome symptoms generally occur within several hours to a few days of concomitant use, presenting as mental status changes (agitation, hallucinations, coma), autonomic instability (tachycardia, hyperthermia, labile blood pressure), and neuromuscular aberrations (hyperreflexia, clonus, rigidity). 
[16] Diagnosis follows the Hunter criteria: spontaneous clonus, inducible clonus plus agitation or diaphoresis, ocular clonus plus agitation or diaphoresis, tremor plus hyperreflexia, or hypertonia plus temperature greater than 38°C plus ocular or inducible clonus. 
[14] Emergency management requires immediate discontinuation of both agents, intravenous fluids, benzodiazepines for agitation and neuromuscular symptoms, external cooling for hyperthermia, and cyproheptadine in moderate to severe cases. 
[14][16]

Long-Term Surveillance and Geriatric Alerts
Structured long-term monitoring must be embedded in the ambulatory workflow. For lithium combined with NSAIDs, lithium levels should be rechecked 5 to 7 days after any NSAID initiation or discontinuation, then every 3 months, with renal function (serum creatinine, eGFR) assessed every 3 to 6 months. 
[6][17] The American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria flag lithium as potentially inappropriate in older adults, especially when combined with drugs impairing renal function. 
[18-19] For patients over 65, lithium therapeutic targets should be lower (0.6 to 0.8 mEq/L rather than 0.8 to 1.2 mEq/L), and monitoring frequency should increase to every 2 to 3 months. 
[20-21] For valproate combined with lamotrigine, liver function tests should be performed every 3 to 6 months, with explicit documentation of skin examination at each visit. 
[17][19] For clozapine combined with benzodiazepines, quarterly reviews for sedation, falls, respiratory function, and cognitive status are required; the Beers Criteria recommend avoiding benzodiazepines in older adults whenever possible and, if used, employing the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration. 
[18-19] For SSRIs combined with tramadol, serum sodium should be checked every 3 to 6 months (tramadol can cause hyponatremia via SIADH), with regular assessment for serotonin syndrome signs and fall risk. 
[18][21] The international consensus list of clinically significant drug-drug interactions in older people validated 66 interactions, with most concerning cardiovascular, antithrombotic, and central nervous system drugs, and recommended treatment modification for three-quarters of identified interactions. 
[17]

Involuntary Admission Criteria and Documentation
Clinical and Legal Criteria
Lei 10.216/2001 establishes that psychiatric hospitalization is exceptional, permitted only when community-based interventions are insufficient. Involuntary admission (internação involuntária) is defined as hospitalization without the patient's consent, at the request of a third party, and requires a clinical diagnosis of mental disorder, evidence of imminent risk to self or others, or severe incapacity for self-care, and documented failure of less restrictive alternatives. 
[25-26] The risk criteria are consistent with international standards: risk to self is the most frequent trigger, with psychotic symptoms, severe mood disorders, and lack of insight commonly present. 
[26-29] The European Court of Human Rights, in the Rooman v. Belgium decision, added that involuntary admission must be accompanied by "real therapeutic measures," reinforcing that the receiving facility must be capable of providing appropriate treatment. 
[30]

The Conselho Federal de Medicina (CFM) Resolution 2.057/2013 operationalizes these criteria, requiring the physician to produce a "laudo médico circunstanciado" that includes the clinical diagnosis (ICD-10/DSM-5 code), description of the patient's mental state, specific risks identified, documentation of failed less restrictive interventions, identification of the third party requesting admission, and the physician's signature, date, and CRM registration number. 
[25] The institution must notify the Ministério Público within 72 hours of involuntary admission. 
[25]

Documentation and Transfer Protocol
For private-to-public transfers, the private clinician's laudo médico circunstanciado accompanies the patient but is not sufficient on its own. The receiving public facility must independently assess the patient, issue its own laudo, and assume responsibility for Ministério Público notification within 72 hours. 
[25][31-32] This dual-laudo requirement ensures legal compliance and independent verification. Brayley et al. found that only 40% of detention forms addressed all legal criteria, underscoring the need for standardized templates that prompt clinicians to document the presence of mental illness, risk to self or others, and the necessity for immediate treatment. 
[31] The Compulsory Treatment Checklist (CTC), developed by Brissos et al., is a validated 25-item instrument (scoring 0 to 50) with four clusters (Legal, Danger, Historic, Cognitive) that achieved 90% accuracy in predicting compulsory treatment, with a cut-off score of 23.5 yielding 75% sensitivity and 93.6% specificity. 
[33-34] The EUNOMIA study recommendations stress providing information to patients about hospitalization reasons, protecting patient rights, encouraging family involvement, and improving communication between community and hospital teams. 
[35]

CAPS III Versus Hospitalization Referral
Risk Stratification and Disposition Criteria
The decision to refer a moderate-to-high suicide risk patient to CAPS III versus psychiatric hospitalization must be guided by evidence-based risk stratification. The Department of Veterans Affairs 2024 guideline for the assessment and management of patients at risk for suicide recommends that patients at high acute risk—defined as those with active suicidal ideation with a specific plan, intent, and access to means, or a recent suicide attempt with ongoing risk factors—require direct observation in an environment with restricted access to lethal means until transfer to a safe setting is feasible. 
[38-39] If psychiatric hospitalization is indicated to maintain safety, local procedures including involuntary admission should be followed. 
[38] The American Academy of Family Physicians concurs that inpatient admission is prudent for patients with a specific plan and means, or when safety cannot be assured in less restrictive settings. 
[40]

Patients at intermediate acute risk may have suicidal ideation with some elements of planning but lack immediate intent or access to means and retain capacity for safety. For these patients, the Department of Veterans Affairs guideline recommends assessing whether the patient can independently maintain safety and whether the benefits of outpatient management outweigh the risks of hospitalization. 
[38] Enhanced care management—including frequent contact, risk reassessment, safety planning, and lethal means counseling—is appropriate if outpatient management is feasible. 
[38][40-41] Fazel and Runeson, writing in the New England Journal of Medicine, emphasize that if the risk of suicide is considered high or uncertain, the person should be referred immediately to mental health services. 
[41] Table 3 from Fazel and Runeson, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, provides a detailed overview of suicide risk and interventions across specific subgroups, including elderly patients, which is directly relevant to disposition decisions in ambulatory practice.

CAPS III units are designed for intensive, community-based crisis intervention, offering 24-hour care with crisis beds for up to 14 days. They are appropriate for patients with moderate-to-high suicide risk who do not require the full containment and medical resources of psychiatric hospitalization but who cannot be safely managed in standard outpatient settings. 
[42-44] Psychiatric hospitalization is reserved for patients at imminent risk, those with active intent and plan, those who have recently attempted suicide and remain at high risk, or those who lack capacity to participate in safety planning. 
[38][40][42]

Operational Pathways and Coverage Transition
CAPS III in São Paulo operates under an "open-door" policy, designed to facilitate rapid access for individuals in psychiatric crisis regardless of their origin within the health system. 
[43-44] The literature supports that private ambulatory psychiatrists can refer patients directly to CAPS III for crisis cases, particularly when the patient is at moderate-to-high suicide risk, provided that the referral is accompanied by appropriate clinical documentation. 
[43-46] While some CAPS III units may require prior registration in UBS or municipal regulation for routine admissions, crisis referrals are typically accommodated without mandatory routing through primary care gateways. 
[43-44][46-47] Direct communication with the CAPS III unit is recommended to confirm bed availability and coordinate care. 
[48]

When a supplementary health (Amil) patient is admitted to CAPS III or UPA, Amil authorization is not required. The patient transitions entirely to SUS coverage for the duration of the crisis episode, as CAPS III and UPA are components of the SUS network and provide care under SUS protocols regardless of supplementary health plan status. 
[49-52] Fontenelle et al. confirmed that privately insured individuals utilize SUS for approximately 13% of their healthcare episodes, particularly for emergency and crisis care. 
[52] Upon discharge, supplementary health plan coverage resumes for outpatient and elective services. 
[49] Operational barriers that may affect this transition include resource constraints and bed shortages, integration gaps between private and public sectors, administrative variability by unit, and stigma. 
[48][53-54] Clinicians should address these barriers through comprehensive documentation, direct communication with receiving units, familiarity with local protocols, and advocacy for equitable access. 
[48][54]

Unified Protocol Integration
The three protocol nodes—drug interaction monitoring, involuntary admission documentation, and CAPS III versus hospitalization referral—must function as an integrated operational framework for private ambulatory psychiatric practice. At the point of prescribing, clinical decision support tools should screen for high-risk drug-drug interactions, with geriatric alerts triggered automatically for patients over 65 based on the American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria and STOPP/START criteria. 
[18-21] Any new prescription, dose change, or discontinuation of a psychotropic or interacting agent should prompt an immediate DDI screen, with explicit documentation of the interaction mechanism, monitoring schedule, and responsible clinician for follow-up. 
[23-24][55] Multidisciplinary team involvement—including pharmacists for drug history reconciliation and nurses for tolerance and adherence assessment—is essential for safe medication management in this population. 
[23][55-56]

When a patient in ambulatory care develops acute signs of a dangerous drug interaction or presents with moderate-to-high suicide risk, the protocol must seamlessly transition from monitoring to emergency response. The clinician should produce a comprehensive clinical summary including risk assessment, current medications, drug interaction concerns, and geriatric-specific flags, which serves as the referral documentation for CAPS III or psychiatric hospitalization. If involuntary admission is indicated, the laudo médico circunstanciado must be completed before transfer, with the understanding that the receiving public facility will issue its own laudo and assume responsibility for Ministério Público notification within 72 hours. 
[25][31-32] The private clinician should communicate directly with the receiving unit by phone or secure electronic means to facilitate continuity of care and clarify the urgency of the referral. 
[35][48]

Comprehensive medication review should be performed at least annually, and after any acute change in clinical status or medication regimen, with explicit documentation of monitoring schedules, deprescribing decisions, and decision points for dose adjustment or discontinuation. 
[20-21][57-58] The 4Ms framework (What Matters, Medication, Mentation, Mobility) supports individualized care plans for older adults, with explicit geriatric flags embedded in the adult protocol that redirect to individualized assessment when age exceeds 65.
